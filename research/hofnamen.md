# The Use of Hofname in NW "Germany"

In Schaumburg-Lippe (and across parts of northwestern Germany), a
**Hofname** (plural: *Hofnamen*) is a "farm name" or "estate name."

Unlike modern hereditary surnames that follow a bloodline, a Hofname was
tied directly to a specific piece of land or farmstead. Here is how the
practice worked:

- **The Property Dictated the Name:** The farm itself had a permanent name.
  Whoever owned, inherited, or managed that specific farm assumed its name
as their legal or social surname, regardless of their birth name.
- **The "Genannt" Practice:** If a man married a woman who inherited a farm
(often because there were no male heirs), he would take her farm's name.
In parish records, you will often see this recorded using the term
**genannt** (meaning "called" or "known as")—for example, *Johann Meier
genannt Krückeberg*.
- **The Challenge for Genealogy:** If a family moved from one farm to
another, their surname would often change to match the new property.
Conversely, two completely unrelated families who lived on the same farm at
different times would share the same Hofname.

The practice of *Hofnamen* (also referred to as *Hofesnamen* or
*Stättenamen*) was common across **northwestern and parts of southern
Germany**. It thrived anywhere agricultural law dictated that an estate had
to be inherited as a single, indivisible unit (implying the name stayed
with the land to protect tax and feudal records).

## Regions with this "Curveball" Naming Practice

### Northwestern Germany (The Core Region)

This was the absolute stronghold of the practice, deeply rooted in
Westphalian and Lower Saxon inheritance customs.

- **Westphalia (*Westfalen*):** Neighbors to Schaumburg-Lippe, this region
  used the system extensively. You will constantly see the *genannt*
(abbreviated *gen.*) or *modo* (Latin for "now") notations in church
records here.
- **Lower Saxony (*Niedersachsen*):** Beyond Schaumburg-Lippe, regions like
  the Emsland, the Osnabrück Land, East Frisia (*Ostfriesland*), and the
Lüneburg Heath regularly practiced it.
- **The Rhineland (*Rheinland*):** Particularly in the northern and eastern
  sections bordering Westphalia, such as the Sauerland and parts of the
Ruhr valley.
- **Schleswig-Holstein:** In some northern marshlands, names were tied
  tightly to the large, established farm estates (*Haubarge*).

### Southern and Southwestern Germany

While less systematic than in the northwest, similar traditions existed
under different regional names.

* **Bavaria (*Bayern*):** Especially in the Alpine regions, Allgäu, and
  Upper Bavaria. Here, the names are often called *Vulgonamen* or
*Hausnamen*. Visually, in records or local speech, people were identified
by their house name first (e.g., *"the [Farm Name] Johann"*).
* **The Black Forest (*Schwarzwald* / Baden-Württemberg):** Because of
  strict undivided inheritance laws to keep mountain farms viable, the
family name often bent to the farm name.

### A Quick Tip for Regional Research

If your research takes you into neighboring Westphalian or Lower Saxon records,
keep an eye out for these specific dialect variations of "genannt" that clerks
used to link a birth name to a farm name:

* **coloni** (Latin for tenant farmer, often followed by the estate name)
* **dictus** / **nominatus** (Latin for "called")
* **gen.** / **gnt.** (German abbreviations for *genannt*)
* **vulgo** (Latin for "commonly known as")
