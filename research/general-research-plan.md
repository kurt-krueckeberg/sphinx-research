## Best target: records created because artisans, Einlieger, and other non-farmholding residents needed permission, regulation, taxation, settlement recognition, or legal action

For rural artisans, Einlieger, and other non-farmholding residents, look for records involving:

* **Schneider**
* **Schuhmacher / Schuster**
* **Handwerker**
* **Professionisten**
* **Zünfte / Ämter**
* **Konzession**
* **Meister**
* **Geselle**
* **Lehrling**
* **Wanderbuch / Kundschaft**
* **Nahrung / Gewerbe**
* **Hausierer / Handel**
* **Einlieger**
* **Inwohner**
* **Beisitzer**
* **Heuerling**
* **Aufenthalt / Niederlassung**
* **Heimatrecht**
* **Armensache / Armenwesen**

The real goal of searching the terms above is to identify **fonds or
series**, not isolated keyword hits. A zero-result search for a term such
as `Einlieger`, `Schneider`, or `Kopfsteuer` does not prove that such
records do not exist; it may only mean that the term does not appear in the
Arcinsys title or description.

The real question becomes:

> Did Schaumburg-Lippe record rural tailors, shoemakers, Einlieger, and other non-farmholding residents through guilds, Amtsakten, concession files, tax/status lists, settlement records, poor-relief files, court/debt records, or police/administrative permissions?

That is a focused archival question.

## Most efficient record classes for your case

### 1. Guild or craft-administration records

If a Berenbusch Krückeberg was a **Schneidermeister** or **Schuhmachermeister**, the best non-church evidence may be in craft regulation records, not parish or land records.

Search terms:

* `Schneider`
* `Schuhmacher`
* `Schuster`
* `Zunft`
* `Amt`
* `Innung`
* `Meister`
* `Geselle`
* `Lehrjunge`
* `Lehrbrief`
* `Meisterstück`

The problem is that these may be cataloged under **Bückeburg**, not Berenbusch. A rural craftsman might appear under a Bückeburg-town craft body or territorial craft regulation file.

### 2. Concession / permission records

A village tailor, shoemaker, Einlieger, or other non-farmholding resident may have needed permission to practice a trade, reside, settle, or maintain an independent household.

Search terms:

* `Konzession`
* `Concession`
* `Erlaubnis`
* `Nahrung`
* `Gewerbe`
* `Profession`
* `Professionist`
* `Handwerk`
* `Nebengewerbe`
* `Aufenthalt`
* `Niederlassung`
* `Heimatrecht`

These are more likely to name individuals than general village files.

### 3. Tax and status lists

Even if they were not farmers, Krückebergs may appear as taxable householders, Einlieger, lodgers, artisans, or occupational taxpayers.

Search terms:

* `Kopfsteuer`
* `Kontribution`
* `Schatzung`
* `Steuer`
* `Einlieger`
* `Inwohner`
* `Beisitzer`
* `Brinksitzer`
* `Heuerling`
* `Professionisten`
* `Berenbusch`
* `Petzen`

These may not prove parentage directly, but they can confirm continuity of residence, identify status, and distinguish same-named men.

### 4. Guardianship, estate, debt, and court files

For artisans and Einlieger, these may be higher yield than land files. Tailors and shoemakers had tools, debts, apprenticeships, household goods, and minor children; Einlieger could appear as debtors, heirs, guardians, litigants, widows, or parents of minor children.

Search terms:

* `Vormundschaft`
* `Curatel / Kuratel`
* `Nachlass`
* `Erbschaft`
* `Teilung`
* `Inventar`
* `Schulden`
* `Concurs / Konkurs`
* `Obligation`
* `Klage`
* `Prozess`

These are more likely to state relationships than tax lists.

### 5. Poor relief / settlement / mobility records

If a rural artisan or Einlieger moved, married outside the parish, became poor, needed permission to reside, or had settlement-right issues, these files can be genealogically valuable.

Search terms:

* `Heimatrecht`
* `Aufenthalt`
* `Niederlassung`
* `Armensache`
* `Armenwesen`
* `Unterstützung`
* `Bettel`
* `Pass`
* `Reisepass`
* `Wanderschaft`

For a shoemaker or tailor, **mobility records** may be especially relevant because training and journeyman movement were normal. For an **Einlieger**, settlement and poor-relief records may be especially relevant because residence and support responsibility could become administrative issues.

## What I would not do

I would not pay the archive to “search for Krückeberg.”

That is too vague and almost guaranteed to waste paid time.

## What I would pay for, if using the 60-minute paid search

Ask them a **finding-aid question**, not a surname question:

> Which fonds or finding aids in NLA Bückeburg contain 18th-century Schaumburg-Lippe records for rural artisans and non-farmholding residents — especially tailors, shoemakers, Einlieger, Inwohner, and Beisitzer — such as guild records, concession records, lists of masters, apprentices, journeymen, occupational taxpayers, tax/status lists, settlement records, poor-relief records, or permissions to practice a trade or reside in villages of the Amt Bückeburg, including Berenbusch/Petzen?

That uses their hour to identify **record systems**, not individual names.

Make a table of every 18th-century Krückeberg in Petzen Kirchenbücher with these columns:

Then identify only the people who are called **Schneider**, **Schuhmacher**, **Schuster**, **Meister**, **Geselle**, **Einlieger**, **Inwohner**, **Beisitzer**, **Brinksitzer**, etc.

After that, search/order records by **occupation + residence status + administrative mechanism**, not by Berenbusch alone.

The key shift is this:

**For farm families, the archival spine is the holding. For the Krückebergs, the archival spine is occupation + residence + legal/social status, including both artisans and Einlieger; Stätte or landholding evidence is relevant only where a specific person is tied to a holding.**
