# Chapter 11 – Navigating the Tektonik: Finding the Right Record Series

In the previous chapter, you learned how to identify **which office** probably created the record you need.

Now you'll learn how to find **where that office's records are located within ARCINSYS**.

This is the practical skill that experienced researchers use every day.

---

# 11.1 What Is the Tektonik?

The **Tektonik** is the archive's organizational tree.

Think of it as the archive's table of contents.

Instead of showing books by subject, it shows:

* archives,
* departments,
* Bestände,
* and record groups.

When you browse the Tektonik, you're exploring the archive exactly as the archivists organized it.

---

# 11.2 Why Browse?

Suppose you search:

> Krückeberg

You find three results.

You browse the appropriate Bestand and discover 150 files covering:

* land sales,
* inheritances,
* mortgages,
* and guardianships.

Only three happened to mention "Krückeberg" in the catalog.

The remaining 147 could still contain references to your family.

Browsing reveals what searching cannot.

---

# 11.3 The Three Levels You Should Learn

Whenever you enter a new Bestand, identify these three things:

### Level 1 – The Office

Who created these records?

Example:

* Amt Bückeburg
* Rentkammer
* Landgericht

---

### Level 2 – The Record Series

What kinds of business did that office conduct?

Examples might include:

* land transactions,
* tax matters,
* inheritance,
* court proceedings,
* emigration.

---

### Level 3 – Individual Files

Now examine the individual files.

This is where your ancestor may appear.

---

# 11.4 Read Before You Click

Many researchers immediately open the first promising file.

Instead:

1. Read the series title.
2. Read its description.
3. Read the dates.
4. Look at neighboring files.

Only then begin opening entries.

You'll often discover a better file than the one that first caught your attention.

---

# 11.5 Follow the Arrangement

Archivists usually arranged files logically.

For example:

```text
1848 Land Sales
1849 Land Sales
1850 Land Sales
1851 Land Sales
```

Or:

```text
Berenbusch
Cammer
Evesen
Petzen
Rusbend
```

Once you recognize the pattern, navigation becomes much faster.

---

# 11.6 Don't Ignore Introductory Notes

Many Bestände begin with introductory information describing:

* the office,
* its history,
* the surviving records,
* missing years,
* and unusual filing practices.

These notes often explain why certain records exist—or why they don't.

Read them before searching deeply.

---

# 11.7 Browse Adjacent Files

Suppose you locate:

> Sale of Holding No. 10, 1849.

Now examine:

* the file immediately before it,
* the file immediately after it.

You may discover:

* another sale in the same village,
* a related inheritance,
* correspondence,
* or additional proceedings involving the same family.

This habit frequently produces unexpected discoveries.

---

# 11.8 Keep a "Map" of the Archive

As you research, make notes like these:

| Bestand       | Contents             | Useful For        |
| ------------- | -------------------- | ----------------- |
| Amt Bückeburg | Local administration | Land, petitions   |
| Rentkammer    | Financial oversight  | Meier holdings    |
| Landgericht   | Court records        | Probate, disputes |

After several months, you'll know the archive almost as well as frequent visitors to the reading room.

---

# 11.9 Think Like an Archivist

Before searching, pause and ask:

> "If I were the clerk responsible for this matter in 1849, where would I have filed these papers?"

That simple exercise often leads you directly to the correct record series.

---

# 11.10 A Practical Exercise

Suppose you learn from a church record that your ancestor inherited a Stätte.

Before opening ARCINSYS, write down:

* Which office supervised inheritances?
* Would court approval have been needed?
* Would the Rentkammer have been involved?
* Would taxes have been reassessed?

Only then begin browsing.

You'll enter ARCINSYS with a research plan instead of relying on trial and error.

---

# Summary

The Tektonik is not merely a navigation tool—it reflects how the archive itself is organized.

The more familiar you become with its structure, the less dependent you'll be on keyword searches.

Eventually, you'll find yourself thinking:

> "I know which office handled this."

And once you know the office, finding the records becomes much easier.
