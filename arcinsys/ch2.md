# Chapter 2 – How German Archives Are Organized

This chapter is the most important in the handbook. If you understand it, ARCINSYS will become much easier to use.

---

# 2.1 Archives Are Not Libraries

A library organizes books by **subject**.

An archive organizes records by **who created them**.

This is called the **principle of provenance** (*Provenienzprinzip*).

For example, suppose the Amt Bückeburg issued 500 documents in 1849.

The archive keeps those records together because they were created by the Amt Bückeburg—not because they concern Berenbusch, Petzen, or the Krückeberg family.

This is why searching only for a surname often misses important records.

---

# 2.2 The Principle of Provenance

Imagine these records concerning Johann Heinrich Krückeberg:

* Sale of Holding No. 10
* Permission to emigrate
* Tax assessment
* Probate
* Military exemption

These were created by different offices.

Therefore they are preserved in different archival groups.

The archive does **not** combine them into one "Krückeberg file."

Instead they remain with the office that created them.

That preserves the historical context.

---

# 2.3 The Archival Hierarchy

Think of the archive as a tree.

```
Archive
│
├── Record Group (Bestand)
│     │
│     ├── Series
│     │      │
│     │      ├── File
│     │      │      │
│     │      │      ├── Individual documents
```

ARCINSYS follows this hierarchy.

You move downward from general to specific.

---

# 2.4 What Is a Bestand?

A **Bestand** is the records created by one office, family, organization, or institution.

Examples include:

* Amt Bückeburg
* Rentkammer
* Landgericht
* Regierung
* Church Consistory

Each becomes its own archival collection.

Think of a Bestand as one large filing cabinet.

---

# 2.5 What Is a Tektonik?

The **Tektonik** is the archive's organizational map.

It shows how every Bestand fits into the archive.

It is similar to a table of contents.

Many beginners ignore the Tektonik.

Experienced researchers use it constantly.

---

# 2.6 What Is a Verzeichnungseinheit?

This simply means one catalogued archival item.

Usually it is:

* one file
* one volume
* one register
* one ledger

It is **not** necessarily a single document.

A Verzeichnungseinheit may contain hundreds of pages.

---

# 2.7 Signaturen

Every archival item receives a unique identifier called a **Signatur**.

Think of it as a call number in a library.

Example:

```
K 2 Nr. 139
```

This tells the archive exactly which file you mean.

Always record Signaturen in your research notes.

---

# 2.8 Laufzeit

The **Laufzeit** is the date span covered by the file.

Example

```
1848–1850
```

This does **not** mean every page was written throughout those years.

It simply indicates the earliest and latest documents.

---

# 2.9 Why Keyword Searches Often Fail

Suppose you search:

```
Krückeberg
```

You may find nothing.

Why?

Because the title of the file might be

> Verkauf einer Stätte in Berenbusch

without mentioning your ancestor.

Or

> Schuldensache betreffend No. 10

Again, no surname.

The surname appears only inside the documents—not in the catalog.

---

# 2.10 Browse Before You Search

This is perhaps the most important research technique.

Instead of asking

> Where is Krückeberg?

ask

> Which office handled this matter?

Then browse that office's records.

For example:

```
Amt Bückeburg

↓

Land Sales

↓

1849

↓

Berenbusch
```

Browsing often succeeds where searching fails.

---

# 2.11 Offices Create Records

Every historical event produces paperwork.

Marriage:

* church
* civil authority

Inheritance:

* court
* Rentkammer
* tax office

Military service:

* recruiting office
* regiment

Emigration:

* government
* police
* customs
* shipping records

Think about **who created the paperwork**.

That tells you where to look.

---

# 2.12 A Schaumburg-Lippe Example

Suppose an ancestor sold Holding No. 18 in Berenbusch before emigrating.

Possible records include:

* application to sell
* creditor proceedings
* appraisal
* purchase contract
* approval by the Rentkammer
* transfer of ownership
* tax adjustment
* emigration permission

These may be preserved in several different Bestände.

The archive keeps each with the office that produced it.

---

# 2.13 Common Beginner Mistakes

**Mistake 1**
Searching only for surnames.

**Better**

Search by office, village, subject, and date.

---

**Mistake 2**

Ignoring the archival hierarchy.

**Better**

Browse the Bestand.

---

**Mistake 3**

Stopping after finding one file.

**Better**

Ask:

"What other offices participated?"

---

**Mistake 4**

Ignoring neighboring files.

Often File 140 is closely related to File 139.

Always examine nearby entries.

---

# Research Habit

Whenever you find one valuable file, ask yourself:

1. Which office created this?

2. Which office received a copy?

3. Which office approved it?

4. Which office recorded the financial consequences?

Those four questions often lead to several additional records.

---

## Summary

The central lesson of this chapter is simple:

**Archives organize records by the office that created them, not by the people named in them.**

Once you begin thinking in terms of administrative offices and their functions, ARCINSYS becomes much more than a searchable catalog—it becomes a map of how historical records were created and preserved.
