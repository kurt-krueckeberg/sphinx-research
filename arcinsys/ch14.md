# Chapter 14 – Major Record Types in the Niedersächsisches Landesarchiv

By now you know how to identify the correct **Bestand** and navigate the **Tektonik**.

The next question is:

> **Which types of records should I search for?**

This chapter explains the major record categories that genealogists encounter in the Niedersächsisches Landesarchiv and, more importantly, **what genealogical questions each can answer**.

---

# 14.1 Church Records Are Only the Beginning

Most genealogists begin with:

* Baptisms
* Marriages
* Burials

These establish the framework of a family.

The archives provide the records that explain the family's legal, financial, and social history.

---

# 14.2 Land Records

**Typical contents**

* Sales
* Purchases
* Mortgages
* Boundary disputes
* Valuations
* Transfers of possession

**Questions answered**

* Which holding did the family occupy?
* When did they acquire it?
* When did they sell it?
* Who owned it before and afterward?
* Why was it transferred?

For Schaumburg-Lippe research, these are among the most valuable records.

---

# 14.3 Court Records

**Typical contents**

* Civil lawsuits
* Probate
* Guardianships
* Debt litigation
* Appeals

**Questions answered**

* Who were the heirs?
* Were minor children involved?
* Were family relationships disputed?
* What property formed the estate?

Court files often identify relatives who never appear together in parish records.

---

# 14.4 Guardianship Records

These arise when minors inherit property or lose one or both parents.

They often identify:

* every surviving child,
* approximate ages,
* guardians,
* close relatives,
* inherited property.

For reconstructing families, guardianship records are exceptionally valuable.

---

# 14.5 Tax Records

Tax records rarely contain dramatic family stories.

Instead they answer practical questions:

* Was the family still living there?
* How valuable was the holding?
* When did ownership change?
* Did the family's financial position improve or decline?

Tax records are particularly useful for filling gaps between parish entries.

---

# 14.6 Rentkammer Records

These concern matters such as:

* hereditary holdings,
* approvals,
* financial administration,
* obligations connected with land.

They are especially important when researching families holding property under **Meierrecht**.

---

# 14.7 Emigration Records

These may include:

* petitions,
* permissions,
* settlement of debts,
* sales of property,
* correspondence with government offices.

Do not expect every emigrant to have a complete file.

However, when one exists, it can explain **why** the family left—not merely **when** they departed.

---

# 14.8 Military Records

Depending on the period, military records may contain:

* recruitment lists,
* service records,
* exemption requests,
* pension material.

They can answer questions such as:

* Did an ancestor serve?
* In which unit?
* Why was he exempted?
* Where was he stationed?

---

# 14.9 Administrative Correspondence

Many offices preserved incoming and outgoing correspondence.

These files may include:

* petitions,
* official reports,
* recommendations,
* approvals,
* complaints.

Although not created for genealogical purposes, they frequently contain names, relationships, occupations, and residences.

---

# 14.10 Maps and Plans

Never overlook maps.

They may show:

* the location of a Hof or Stätte,
* property boundaries,
* neighboring holdings,
* roads,
* streams,
* field names.

Maps can often be correlated with written records to reconstruct the physical setting in which your ancestors lived.

---

# 14.11 Which Record Should I Search First?

Use this guide:

| Research Question                            | Start With                          |
| -------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Who inherited the holding?                   | Court or inheritance records        |
| Why was the property sold?                   | Land records and Rentkammer         |
| Why did the family emigrate?                 | Emigration files, then land records |
| Who occupied the holding before my ancestor? | Land records                        |
| Were there minor children?                   | Guardianship records                |
| Was there litigation?                        | Court records                       |

Notice that the answer is almost never "search the surname."

Instead, begin with the **type of event**.

---

# 14.12 Think Like an Investigator

Each record answers one part of the story.

For example:

Church register:

> The owner died.

Court record:

> The heirs were identified.

Guardianship file:

> The children were minors.

Land record:

> The holding was transferred.

Rentkammer file:

> The transfer was approved.

Viewed together, these separate records reconstruct the complete event.

---

# Summary

The Niedersächsisches Landesarchiv preserves many different categories of records because many different government offices created them.

The most successful genealogists don't search every record type randomly. They first identify the historical event they are investigating and then select the record types most likely to document that event.
