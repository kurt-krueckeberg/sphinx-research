# Chapter 9 – Researching a Hof or Stätte Through Time

One of the greatest advantages of archival research in Schaumburg-Lippe is that you are often **not limited to following people**. Instead, you can follow a **specific holding**—a Hof or Stätte—through decades or even centuries.

This chapter explains how.

---

# 9.1 Change Your Perspective

Most genealogists think like this:

> "I'm researching Johann Heinrich Krückeberg."

Instead, ask:

> "I'm researching Berenbusch No. 10."

That small change often transforms your research.

People die.

Widows remarry.

Children inherit.

Surnames change.

Hofnamen change.

The holding remains.

---

# 9.2 Begin with the Known

Suppose you know:

* Village: Berenbusch
* Holding: No. 10
* Year: 1849

That is enough to begin reconstructing the history of the holding.

---

# 9.3 Build a Timeline

Create a chronological timeline.

For example:

| Year | Event              | Source          |
| ---- | ------------------ | --------------- |
| 1761 | Marriage of holder | Parish register |
| 1784 | Child baptized     | Parish register |
| 1822 | Holder dies        | Burial register |
| 1849 | Holding sold       | Amt file        |
| 1849 | Emigration         | Passenger list  |

As additional records are found, expand the timeline.

Soon the history of the holding becomes clear.

---

# 9.4 Record Every Holder

Instead of recording only your direct ancestor, list **every known occupant**.

For example:

| Dates     | Holder                           |
| --------- | -------------------------------- |
| ?–1822    | Johann Heinrich Krückeberg       |
| 1822–1849 | Johann Heinrich Krückeberg (son) |
| 1849–     | Wilhelm Bornemann                |

Even families unrelated by blood become important because they explain later records.

---

# 9.5 Use Church Records

Church registers help establish:

* births,
* marriages,
* deaths,
* sponsors,
* occupations,
* residence.

Whenever possible, connect each event to the holding.

Instead of writing:

> Johann Heinrich Krückeberg married...

write:

> Holder of Berenbusch No. 10 married...

That keeps the property at the center of your research.

---

# 9.6 Add Archival Records

Now begin adding:

* land sales,
* inheritance,
* mortgages,
* tax assessments,
* court cases,
* Rentkammer approvals.

These explain **why** changes occurred.

---

# 9.7 Pay Attention to Neighbors

Land records frequently identify adjoining holdings.

Those neighboring families often appear as:

* witnesses,
* sponsors,
* creditors,
* guardians,
* purchasers.

Entire village networks begin to emerge.

---

# 9.8 Recognize Patterns

After studying several holdings, recurring patterns become apparent.

For example:

* inheritance by one heir,
* sale immediately before emigration,
* repeated interaction with particular officials,
* neighboring families serving as witnesses.

Recognizing these patterns makes future research easier.

---

# 9.9 Don't Stop at One Generation

Suppose your ancestor acquired a holding in 1780.

Ask:

Who held it before?

Who inherited it afterward?

How long did that family remain?

Researching one generation often uncovers three or four more.

---

# 9.10 Hofnamen

In regions where **Hofnamen** were used, following the holding becomes even more important.

The Hofname may remain attached to the property even after the surname of the occupants changes.

Conversely, the same surname may appear on different holdings.

Understanding the distinction prevents many identification errors.

---

# 9.11 Case Study

Imagine researching Evesen No. 20.

Rather than collecting isolated baptism records, you compile:

* every holder,
* every marriage,
* every inheritance,
* every sale,
* every Rentkammer file,
* every emigration.

After several years, you possess not merely a family tree but a documented history of the holding.

---

# 9.12 A Research Worksheet

For each Hof or Stätte, maintain a separate worksheet containing:

**Identification**

* Village
* House number
* Hofname (if known)

**Known Holders**

* Dates
* Names
* Relationship

**Church Records**

* Baptisms
* Marriages
* Burials

**Archival Records**

* Signaturen
* Titles
* Dates

**Notes**

* Neighboring properties
* Witnesses
* Guardians
* Creditors

This worksheet becomes the central reference for all future research.

---

# 9.13 The Goal

Ultimately, you want to answer questions such as:

* Who occupied this holding?
* When did ownership change?
* Why did it change?
* How was it inherited?
* Which families were connected with it?

Those questions often reveal relationships that parish registers alone never disclose.

---

# Summary

A Hof or Stätte is more than a place where an ancestor lived.

It is a continuous thread running through generations.

By following the history of the holding—not just the family—you gain a deeper understanding of both.
