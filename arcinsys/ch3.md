# Chapter 3 – Navigating ARCINSYS

In this chapter, you'll learn how to move through ARCINSYS efficiently. Most new users rely almost entirely on the search box. Experienced researchers often do the opposite: they use the archival hierarchy (the **Tektonik**) to browse.

---

# 3.1 The Home Page

When you open ARCINSYS, you'll generally see:

* A search box
* The archive selection
* The **Tektonik** (archival hierarchy)
* Menus for advanced search and help

Don't think of the search box as your primary tool. Think of it as one of several tools.

---

# 3.2 Choosing the Correct Archive

Before searching, make sure you're in the correct archive.

For your research, this is often the **Niedersächsisches Landesarchiv**, particularly the **Abteilung Bückeburg**, because it holds many records of the former Principality of Schaumburg-Lippe.

Searching the wrong archive may produce no results even though the records exist.

---

# 3.3 Simple Search

A simple search is useful for:

* uncommon surnames,
* village names,
* exact file titles,
* or known Signaturen.

For example:

* `Krückeberg`
* `Berenbusch`
* `Rentkammer`
* `K 2`

Start broadly. If you get too many results, narrow the search.

---

# 3.4 Why Searches Fail

Suppose you search for **Johann Heinrich Krückeberg**.

No results.

Does that mean the archive has no records?

No.

The file may be titled:

> Verkauf einer Stätte in Berenbusch

or

> Schuldensache Nr. 10

Your ancestor appears only inside the documents, not in the catalog description.

Never assume that "no search results" means "no records."

---

# 3.5 Browsing the Tektonik

The Tektonik is a tree showing how the archive is organized.

Instead of searching, you expand branches until you reach the office and series you're interested in.

A simplified example:

```text
Niedersächsisches Landesarchiv
└── Abteilung Bückeburg
    └── Amt Bückeburg
        └── Grundstücksangelegenheiten
            └── Verkäufe
                └── 1849
```

You are moving from the general to the specific.

---

# 3.6 Reading a Catalog Entry

A typical entry includes:

* **Title** – a brief description of the file.
* **Signatur** – the archival reference.
* **Laufzeit** – the date range.
* **Scope/content** (when available).
* **Digitized?** – whether images are available online.

Read the entire entry. Important clues often appear in the description, not just the title.

---

# 3.7 Digital Images

Some files have digitized images.

If they do:

* check that all pages are present,
* note the page numbering,
* and download or record the Signatur along with the image information.

If there are no images, the catalog entry is still valuable because it tells you the file exists.

---

# 3.8 Following Leads

One file often points to another.

For example, a petition might state:

> "Submitted to the Rentkammer for approval."

That tells you another office created additional records.

This is one of the most powerful research techniques in archival work: use one file to discover another.

---

# 3.9 Searching by Office Instead of Person

Instead of asking:

> "Where is Johann Heinrich Krückeberg?"

Ask:

> "Which office handled the sale of a Stätte in 1849?"

That shift in thinking frequently uncovers records that a surname search never would.

---

# 3.10 Keeping Research Notes

For every useful file, record:

* Archive
* Bestand
* Signatur
* Title
* Laufzeit
* Whether it is digitized
* Notes about related offices or files

Good notes prevent you from repeating searches and make it easier to trace related records later.

---

# Summary

The search box is useful, but it is not the heart of ARCINSYS.

The heart of ARCINSYS is the archival hierarchy. The more you learn to browse by office, administrative function, and record series, the more successful your research will become.
