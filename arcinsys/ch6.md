# Chapter 6 – Understanding Signatures, Record Citations, and Archival References

If you spend much time in ARCINSYS, you'll quickly encounter references such as:

```
K 2 Nr. 139
```

or

```
N.C. 4386
```

To a beginner, these look cryptic. To an experienced researcher, they are valuable clues.

---

# 6.1 What Is a Signatur?

A **Signatur** is the archive's unique identifier for a file or volume.

It serves the same purpose as a library call number.

Every archival item has one.

If you request a file in the reading room, the Signatur—not the title—is what the staff needs.

---

# 6.2 Why Signatures Matter

Never write in your notes:

> "Found a file about Berenbusch."

Instead record:

* Archive
* Bestand
* Signatur
* Title
* Date
* URL (if available)

Years later, you'll be able to locate the exact file again.

---

# 6.3 A Typical Citation

A complete citation usually includes:

* Archive
* Department (if applicable)
* Bestand
* Signatur
* File title
* Date

For example:

> Niedersächsisches Landesarchiv, Abteilung Bückeburg, K 2 Nr. 139, *Verkauf einer Stätte in Berenbusch*, 1849.

This is enough for another researcher to locate the same file.

---

# 6.4 Signatures Are Identifiers—Not Descriptions

One common mistake is trying to interpret every part of a Signatur.

Sometimes the letters indicate a record group or historical classification.

Sometimes they reflect an older cataloging system.

Their exact meaning varies by archive and by collection.

Your first concern is simply to recognize that the Signatur uniquely identifies the file.

---

# 6.5 Historical Catalog Numbers

Many archives retained older numbering systems when they adopted ARCINSYS.

As a result, you may encounter references such as:

* K 2
* N.C.
* L
* Rep.

These are often inherited from earlier finding aids and inventories.

Knowing that can help you connect older publications with modern catalog entries.

---

# 6.6 One File Can Reference Another

Suppose a file contains this statement:

> "See also N.C. 4386."

That is not just a citation.

It is a research lead.

Always record cross-references and look up the related file.

Archives frequently preserve different parts of the same event in separate files.

---

# 6.7 File Numbers Often Reflect Arrangement

Imagine you find:

* Nr. 138
* Nr. 139
* Nr. 140

These may have been cataloged together because they belong to the same series.

Although the subjects differ, neighboring files are often worth examining.

This is especially true for:

* land sales,
* court proceedings,
* tax records,
* and administrative correspondence.

---

# 6.8 Don't Invent Meaning

Researchers sometimes assume that every letter or number has a precise historical meaning.

Often it does not.

Sometimes a code simply reflects how the archive arranged its holdings decades ago.

If you don't know what a designation means, treat it as an identifier unless the finding aid explains otherwise.

---

# 6.9 Recording Citations in Your Notes

I recommend using a consistent format.

For example:

```
Archive:
Niedersächsisches Landesarchiv, Abteilung Bückeburg

Bestand:
K 2

Signatur:
Nr. 139

Title:
Verkauf einer Stätte in Berenbusch

Date:
1849

Notes:
Mentions Rentkammer approval.
```

This format makes it easy to return to the file later.

---

# 6.10 A Real Example from Your Research

In your research on the sale of **Holding No. 10 in Berenbusch**, the Signatur identified the file documenting the transaction.

The value of that Signatur extended beyond locating the file itself. It allowed you to:

* identify the exact archival item,
* cite it accurately,
* compare it with nearby files,
* and use references within it to locate related records, such as those created by the Rentkammer.

This illustrates an important principle:

> A Signatur is not the end of your research—it is often the beginning of the next stage.

---

# Summary

Always record complete archival citations.

Treat every Signatur as a permanent reference to a specific archival item, and pay close attention to cross-references and neighboring files. They frequently lead to additional discoveries that a keyword search alone would never reveal.
