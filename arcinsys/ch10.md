# Chapter 10 – Finding the Correct Bestand: The Most Important Skill in ARCINSYS

If you remember only one chapter from this handbook, make it this one.

Most unsuccessful searches in ARCINSYS are not caused by poor search terms—they're caused by searching the **wrong Bestand**.

---

# 10.1 Stop Asking "Where Is My Ancestor?"

Instead of asking:

> "Where is Johann Heinrich Krückeberg?"

Ask:

> "Which government office would have created a record of this event?"

Archives preserve **records created by offices**, not files about people.

---

# 10.2 Start With the Event

Every genealogical question begins with an event.

Examples:

* Baptism
* Marriage
* Death
* Sale of a Stätte
* Inheritance
* Emigration
* Guardianship
* Military service

Now ask:

> **Who had legal responsibility for that event?**

That office is your starting point.

---

# 10.3 Work Backwards

Suppose your question is:

> "Why did my ancestor leave Schaumburg-Lippe in 1849?"

Don't search for the surname first.

Instead think:

Possible records:

* permission to emigrate
* sale of property
* creditor proceedings
* tax settlement

Possible offices:

* Amt Bückeburg
* Rentkammer
* Court
* Financial authorities

Only after identifying the offices do you begin searching ARCINSYS.

---

# 10.4 Build an "Office Tree"

For every research problem, make a list.

Example:

**Problem**

Sale of a hereditary Stätte.

**Possible offices**

✓ Amt Bückeburg

✓ Rentkammer

✓ Court

✓ Tax office

That immediately suggests four Bestände to investigate.

---

# 10.5 One Event Creates Many Records

Think of an event as throwing a stone into a pond.

The event is the stone.

The paperwork spreads outward in circles.

Example:

A sale of a Hof may produce:

* petition
* valuation
* correspondence
* approval
* purchase contract
* tax adjustment

Different offices create different parts.

Your task is to locate all of them.

---

# 10.6 Use the Tektonik

Once you've identified the office:

Don't search.

Browse.

Expand the Tektonik until you reach that office's Bestand.

Then examine its series.

This is often faster than repeated keyword searches.

---

# 10.7 Learn Administrative History

One of the biggest differences between beginners and experienced researchers is knowledge of historical government.

For example, if you know that the Rentkammer supervised hereditary holdings, you'll naturally examine its records when researching a Meierhof.

Without that knowledge, you might never think to look there.

The better you understand nineteenth-century administration, the easier ARCINSYS becomes.

---

# 10.8 Read the Scope Notes

Many Bestände begin with a description explaining:

* what the office did,
* what records survive,
* what years are covered,
* and sometimes what has been lost.

Read these notes carefully.

They often tell you exactly whether the Bestand is relevant.

---

# 10.9 Don't Stop With the First Bestand

Suppose you locate the land-sale file.

You're not finished.

Ask:

Who approved it?

Who collected taxes?

Who recorded the inheritance?

Who handled any appeal?

Each answer points to another office—and another Bestand.

---

# 10.10 A Practical Example

Suppose you know only this:

> Johann Heinrich Krückeberg sold Holding No. 10 in 1849.

An inexperienced researcher searches:

```
Krückeberg
```

An experienced researcher thinks:

* Sale of hereditary holding.
* Which office handled sales?
* Was approval required?
* Which financial office became involved?
* Were creditors notified?
* Did the owner emigrate afterward?

Those questions naturally lead to several archival collections, not just one.

---

# 10.11 Your Research Notebook

Create a page titled:

**Government Offices**

As you research, add entries such as:

| Office        | What it handled                       | Relevant Bestände |
| ------------- | ------------------------------------- | ----------------- |
| Amt Bückeburg | Local administration, land, petitions | ...               |
| Rentkammer    | Hereditary holdings, approvals        | ...               |
| Landgericht   | Litigation, inheritance               | ...               |

Over time, this becomes your personal guide to the archive.

---

# 10.12 The Golden Rule

When you cannot find a record, do **not** immediately assume it has been lost.

Instead ask:

> **Am I looking in the Bestand of the office that would actually have created it?**

That single question solves an astonishing number of "missing record" problems.

---

# Chapter Summary

The key to successful archival research is identifying the **record-creating office** before you search.

Think in this order:

1. What happened?
2. Which office handled it?
3. Which Bestand contains that office's records?
4. Which series within that Bestand is most likely to contain the file?

If you consistently follow those four steps, you'll search ARCINSYS much more effectively than researchers who rely only on the search box.
