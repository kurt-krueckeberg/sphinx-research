# Chapter 13 – Using Older Printed Finding Aids and Inventories

One of the most overlooked research techniques is using **finding aids that were created before ARCINSYS existed**.

Many archival collections were described decades ago in printed inventories, typed finding aids, or card catalogs. When ARCINSYS was introduced, much of that information was imported, but not always in its entirety.

An experienced researcher learns to use both.

---

# 13.1 What Is an Older Finding Aid?

Before computerized catalogs, archivists described their holdings in:

* printed inventories,
* typed finding aids,
* handwritten registers,
* card catalogs.

Many are still valuable today because they often contain descriptions that are more detailed than the ARCINSYS entry.

---

# 13.2 Why They Matter

An ARCINSYS entry may read:

> Sale of a Stätte in Berenbusch.

An older finding aid might add:

* the seller,
* the purchaser,
* the house number,
* or the reason for the transaction.

That additional detail can save hours of searching.

---

# 13.3 Don't Assume ARCINSYS Contains Everything

ARCINSYS is the primary catalog, but it is not always the complete description of every collection.

Some older finding aids include:

* historical notes,
* explanations of filing systems,
* obsolete signatures,
* cross-references,
* and comments omitted from the online catalog.

Whenever an older inventory exists, it is worth consulting.

---

# 13.4 Understanding Older Signatures

You may encounter citations in books or articles that do not exactly match modern ARCINSYS references.

For example:

* older shelf marks,
* earlier Bestand designations,
* or historical file numbers.

Don't assume the citation is obsolete or useless.

Instead, use it as a clue and compare it with the modern archival description. Archives often preserve references to former signatures.

---

# 13.5 Read the Introduction

Many printed finding aids begin with an introduction explaining:

* the history of the office,
* how the records were created,
* what has survived,
* what has been lost,
* and how the records are arranged.

These introductions are often excellent guides for genealogists because they explain **why** the records exist.

---

# 13.6 Administrative History Matters

Suppose an office changed its responsibilities in 1820.

Records created before that year may be in one Bestand.

Records created afterward may be in another.

A printed inventory often explains such changes, helping you avoid searching the wrong collection.

---

# 13.7 Using Older Inventories Together with ARCINSYS

A practical approach is:

1. Locate the relevant Bestand in ARCINSYS.
2. Determine whether an older finding aid exists.
3. Read its introduction.
4. Compare the older descriptions with the online entries.
5. Record any older signatures or cross-references.

Using both resources usually gives a fuller understanding than relying on either one alone.

---

# 13.8 Publications by Local Archives

Many archives have published inventories or guides describing specific collections.

These publications may discuss:

* village records,
* court records,
* noble estates,
* financial administration,
* or church archives.

Although they are not finding aids in the formal sense, they often explain the records far better than a catalog entry.

---

# 13.9 Research Habit

Whenever you identify an important Bestand, ask:

* Was a printed inventory published?
* Does the archive provide a PDF finding aid?
* Is there an introduction explaining the collection?

Five minutes spent reading that material can save hours of unsuccessful searching.

---

# 13.10 Example

Suppose you're researching the **Amt Bückeburg**.

Before searching individual files:

* read the introduction describing the Amt,
* learn its responsibilities,
* understand how its records are arranged,
* identify the major series.

Only then begin searching.

You'll know where to look—and equally important, where **not** to look.

---

# Summary

Older finding aids are not obsolete.

They are often the key to understanding the organization of a Bestand and can provide details that are abbreviated or omitted in ARCINSYS.

For serious genealogical research, use **both** the online catalog and the earlier finding aids whenever they are available.
