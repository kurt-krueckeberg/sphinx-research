# Part 10

There are also small Kolonen (*hereditary farmholders*) in Schaumburg-Lippe
— Brinksitzer (*smallholders on the edge of the village or common*) — whose
condition is usually worse than that of the Einlieger (*resident landless
tenants/lodgers*). Among the Einlieger there are often relatively
well-provided people who at the same time have only insignificant burdens
to bear, whereas the small Kolonen, as owners, are already drawn more
heavily into the general and communal burdens.

Spinning and weaving have remained more of a secondary occupation in
Schaumburg-Lippe. For that reason, the decline of the linen industry has
been able to have a less harmful effect here than in regions where the
landless rural population was wholly dependent on it. Only in the Amt
Hagenburg (*administrative district of Hagenburg*), especially around
Steinhude, is weaving done for larger-scale sale; otherwise it is done only
for individual small orders.

The cultivation of flax and its preparation have, on the whole, remained
very backward. Manuring, sowing, treatment in the field, retting, breaking,
heckling — in short, the whole treatment until the thread reaches the
spinning wheel — leaves very much to be desired. There has as yet been
little talk here of changing this treatment, though people are already
moving toward such change in other regions.

Far less than in Lippe-Detmold do the Einlieger in Schaumburg-Lippe occupy
themselves with work abroad. Hollandsgehen (*seasonal labor migration to
Holland or abroad*) for mowing and also for herring fishing has greatly
declined. The workers go more to larger construction projects outside the
country, for example to Hamburg after the fire, to Bremerhaven, and so
forth, and to field brickyards in the Landdrostei Stade (*Hanoverian
administrative district of Stade*) and in Brandenburg.

That there is also much opportunity for work for the Einlieger within the
country itself, and indeed more than in many regions, hardly needs mention.
Mining alone employs a not insignificant number of people throughout the
entire year. Less significant than in the Hessian part of the former
county, in the stone quarries on the Bückeberg, are the works in the stone
quarries on the Harrl. The extensive forests and road construction also
provide many kinds of employment.

One principal labor of the Einlieger is the Dienste (*labor services*). In
the Principality of Schaumburg-Lippe, the redemption of manorial dues and
services has not yet taken place. For the larger Kolonen, the Einlieger
customarily take over the Handdienste (*manual labor services*) for
payment, or, as people say, perform the Diensttage (*service days*).
Usually these larger Kolonen stand with the Dienstbauern (*service peasants
owing manorial labor*) on such a footing that specific Einlieger are
summoned in their place, often from other Kolonate and in other villages.

The Einlieger do not contribute to the communal burdens. The obligation to
contribute to road construction through Landfolgen (*territorial compulsory
labor obligations*) is redeemed annually by most Kolonen through payment of
four Contributionssimplen (*basic contribution assessments*). The country
roads are therefore usually repaired and maintained by day laborers, who
earn 8 Sgr. per day. This work can likewise be regarded as one of the main
sources of income of the Einlieger.

It is of very great advantage to the Einlieger that in Schaumburg-Lippe the
Gemeinheiten (*common lands*) have remained undivided up to now. Their
livestock is driven onto them together with the others’ livestock in return
for a herdsman’s fee.

If in other regions there are frequent complaints about luxury in clothing
even among the lower estates, especially that glittering finery is
spreading very widely, then in contrast Schaumburg-Lippe, as indeed the
adjoining region generally, must be praised because the old “defftige”
Tracht (*sturdy, traditional rural dress*) has been preserved here, as
people say in Westphalia. This has had the most beneficial consequences for
the landless population.

As in few regions, perhaps, this peasant costume has defied every fashion
and all changes, even the slightest, in cut and fabric. In its completeness
— for which, however, the poorest servant and the poorest maid strive — it
is by no means cheap, but rather very expensive. Yet for that reason it is
also extraordinarily durable, and every garment that takes dirt is
washable.

If anywhere, then in this clothing there is Deftigkeit (*solidity, sturdy
plainness*). It is the same for summer and winter. Only when it is cold are
all the layers worn together, whereas one or another is left off depending
on whether it is more or less warm. When all the layers are together, the
clothing is warm; yet it is thoroughly suited for work, light, cooling, and
comfortable when the layers have been removed.

On high summer days, for example when haymaking, the young man wears a
shirt and his three-quarter trousers, and perhaps puts on only a sleeved
waistcoat when going to and from the work. If wood is hauled in winter,
then two shirts, two pairs of trousers, two to three sleeved waistcoats —
no other kind is known — and a white linen lined smock are worn, and the
trimming of the fur cap is folded down over the neck.

In a similar way, according to the season, the various layers are added or
left off by the women. Among them there is a special custom: the bright red
skirts that are usual for ordinary churchgoing are exchanged for black or
brown ones at the celebration of the Lord’s Supper. A blue cloth coat with
broad skirts and large covered buttons is the man’s clothing for
churchgoing and for funerals. Usually the coat is acquired at marriage and
then lasts for the whole of life. Lack of church clothing is regarded as
poverty.

We hardly need add that the excellent, solid costume of the peasant estate
has a beneficial effect on its inner character and preserves the Einlieger
from many evils with which the Heuerleute (*tenant laborers*) in a large
part of our Principality of Osnabrück have to struggle. For here,
especially in the north, the old national costume has already almost
entirely disappeared, whereby the native clothing materials have at the
same time been displaced. This has brought unspeakable disadvantages to the
manually laboring class, which has now become dependent on changing
fashion.

We can therefore only wish that in Schaumburg-Lippe, as everywhere where a
national costume is still found among the peasant estate, it may preserve
itself through all times and help to preserve this estate in its
characteristically definite nature, and to protect it from a general
leveling, with which estate custom and honor disappear.
