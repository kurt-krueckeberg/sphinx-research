## I. Lippe-Detmold

According to the census of 1846 (*Zeitschrift für deutsche Statistik*,
1847, p. 1059), the Principality of Lippe-Detmold supports 106,046
inhabitants on 20.6 square miles.

In 1834 the population numbered 98,428 (97,720), and by 1840 it had risen
to 104,462, so that one may assume an average annual population increase of
0.7 percent. On average, there are 5,148 inhabitants per square mile. This
is a population density found in only a few German territories. Only the
Kingdom of Saxony, with 6,760 inhabitants per square mile; the Grand Duchy
of Hesse, with 5,578 inhabitants per square mile; the Duchy of
Saxe-Altenburg, with 5,367 inhabitants per square mile; and the
Principality of Reuss-Greiz, with 5,170 inhabitants per square mile, are
more densely populated. If we now consider that the Principality of Lippe
is by no means fertile and cultivated throughout, but rather, being
mountainous, often resists cultivation, and that it also contains large
forests and many stretches of heath and only small towns, we must already
conclude from this that conditions of a very peculiar kind must exist
there, through which it becomes possible for the land to support so dense a
population, especially since commerce, like the factories, is not of great
importance. These very peculiar conditions will confront us when we examine
the situation of the so-called Einlieger (landless rural lodger or tenant
living in another household or on another holding), who were mentioned not
long ago in this journal, p. 639, as not having voting rights in communal
affairs.

### 1. Origin of the Einlieger

The landless rural population in the Principality of Lippe has increased
since the end of the previous century to such a degree that, just as is the
case in the Principality of Osnabrück, it exceeds the landowning
population, on average, by a factor of two. Just as among us these landless
rural inhabitants are called Heuerleute or Heuerlinge, from *heuern*, that
is, to rent or hire for one year — *heuer* meaning “this year” — so in
Lippe they are called Einlieger, because they lodge in with others, that
is, live in another person’s household or dwelling. These Einlieger, to
whom at present roughly two thirds of the entire rural population belong,
were found in the Principality of Lippe until the middle of the previous
century only in very small numbers, and in some districts perhaps not at
all. Until then the younger sons of holding owners, who did not have the
opportunity to marry onto other holdings or to enter into other
circumstances, either settled on a still-uncultivated site in a village, or
they built for themselves on the Gemeinheiten (plural of Gemeinheit, common
land or village common) belonging to the village, also called heaths. In
this way many small landowners arose; indeed, when the Gemeinheiten were
gradually cultivated, even small settlements were formed, which were then
usually named after the villages or estates from which the colonization had
proceeded: Hölser-, Herser-, Wülferheide, and so forth. In general, in
Lippe there predominates the inclination to live together in villages and
not in Bauerschaften (rural townships or farming districts), as is more the
case in Westphalia proper, where the holdings are then separated from one
another by greater or lesser distances. Meanwhile, the Lippe villages are
not closed villages in the way already found where the Engern and
Eastphalian pattern, in contrast to the Westphalian, appears in its pure
form.

The smaller landowners who arose later — Neubauer (new settlers), Neuwohner
(new inhabitants), or, as the vernacular calls them, Hoppenplöcker —
possessed before the division of the Gemeinheiten, with few exceptions
apart from a little garden land, which was probably assigned to them at
once when they received permission to build, virtually no land at all. At
first they supported themselves by day labor on the holdings, where they
also hired out their children as servants. As the population increased
further, the work they found within the country was no longer sufficient
for their maintenance; they therefore sought work abroad and, like their
neighbors in Westphalia proper, turned toward Holland, West Friesland,
Groningen, and the adjoining regions. It is well known that in those times,
when commerce in the United Netherlands was significant, manual labor
brought rich earnings there, since labor was lacking. As soon as the sons
had grown up, they followed their fathers. Through this temporary
emigration of the working population during the summer, a shortage of
workers arose in their homeland, and especially in the season when the
holdings particularly needed outside help. Just as among us in the
Osnabrück region, the holding owners often complained about this and also
appealed to the territorial government to set limits on this temporary
emigration. Möser says with reference to this that the landowners had
believed the state existed for their sake. What wonder, then, that they
thought the state must supply them, by prohibiting the going to Holland,
with workers who would serve for low wages? Incidentally, as in the
Osnabrück region, so also in Lippe, the territorial government did not
reject the complaints raised on this account, but recognized them as
justified. With respect to Lippe, we admittedly know of no specific
ordinance to cite; but we have been told that such ordinances were issued,
though without success. Matters will not have been different here from what
they were in our former prince-bishopric. Bishop Philipp Sigismund already
expressed himself in 1608 to the estates to the effect that complaints had
been received about servants and laborers, that no one wished to serve,
whereby the hereditary peasants and the burghers were placed in difficulty;
rather, the unmarried people went off according to their own pleasure to
Friesland and elsewhere outside the bishopric, where they could earn more
in a shorter time, in order later to return again according to their own
pleasure; further, the payment of laborers was excessive, and so forth, for
which reason it was to be carefully considered whether a police ordinance
should not be issued for such cases. Although we are of the opinion that in
earlier times there were still many sources of livelihood within the
country itself, it was in any case wrong to try to restrict earning a
livelihood abroad by police measures, merely so that the settled
inhabitants might obtain cheap day laborers and servants. It was only
natural that these measures bore no fruit.

The consequence of this temporary emigration was, in Lippe, the emergence
of the so-called Einlieger. In order to obtain workers, the holding owners,
as the population increased, gradually began to take married persons into
their dwellings on a rental basis — to let them lodge in with them — and
indeed on the condition that, when required, they would work for a fixed
daily wage. Separate dwellings for the Einlieger did not exist at first.
The Leibzuchtshäuser (retirement houses reserved for the older generation
on a holding) that happened to stand empty were used first. Later, however,
special houses, usually arranged for two families, had to be built. They
are now found on almost all Kolonate (plural of Kolonat, registered rural
holding or tenement; not necessarily a farm). The extent to which this
landless population of Einlieger has increased in Lippe in more recent
times may be shown by the following example: a holding owner informs me
that in the year 1772, in one village, when his grandfather married onto
the holding now possessed by him, there were only five or six Einlieger
there; in 1846, by contrast, when he prepared the list of Einlieger, their
number had risen to 110. The number of landowners, on the other hand, was
relatively small; among 39 there were only 10 larger Kolonen (plural of
Kolon, holder of a Kolonat or registered rural holding; not necessarily a
farmer in the modern sense), who kept four or more horses, and 10 others,
each of whom cultivated 20 to 60 Lippe Scheffelsaat (a seed-measure of
land, roughly the area sown with one Scheffel of seed), in part with cows;
the rest were small owners, Hoppenplöcker, who possessed no other pieces of
land than the portions of the Gemeinheiten that had fallen to them or even
only their little gardens; in addition, there was also a noble estate
there.

As a consequence of the increase of the Einlieger, one must regard the
division of the Gemeinheiten that took place throughout. The Einlieger
namely drove their livestock without further ado onto the common pastures,
so that these, especially since Lippe as a whole possesses only little
pastureland, were excessively grazed by livestock and yielded little as
Hude (common grazing land or grazing right). Not without justification did
the small landowners complain that the larger ones, with their own
livestock and the additional livestock of their Einlieger, were making
proportionally too great a use of the Gemeinheit. These constant complaints
became the occasion for dividing the Gemeinheiten almost everywhere,
gradually, in order to be rid of the matter. Given the dense population of
the land and the small benefit which, in general, the Gemeinheiten brought
in this poorly watered region, the division may have been appropriate
insofar as new land for cultivation was gained through it, even though the
condition of the Einlieger was not improved by it. We are admittedly not in
favor of unconditional division of the Gemeinheiten, as it has mostly taken
place in the Principality of Osnabrück without any need for it, but believe
rather that a limited division, such as was appropriate to the increased
population, would have brought greater advantages; nevertheless we readily
acknowledge that in Lippe, where such large Gemeinheiten as are found here
among us did not exist at all, the circumstances were essentially
different. Since the terrain in Lippe is generally not very suitable for
pasture, the divided Gemeinheiten, if they are cultivated, will certainly
on average yield twice as much as before. Some places will of course remain
that either resist cultivation entirely or yield a return only when already
existing productive forces can be directed into them in order to awaken new
ones.

### 2. The Sources of Livelihood of the Einlieger

The manual-laboring classes in the Principality of Lippe, among whom, in
addition to the Einlieger, one must also count the small landowners who
live under the same conditions, support themselves chiefly through the
linen industry and then through brickmaking work abroad.

**a.** In the already cited work *On the Condition of the Heuerleute*,
etc., we expressed ourselves on the decline of the linen industry in
general. What we communicated there also applies to Lippe; at present, even
with all diligence, a manual-laboring family can no longer obtain its
livelihood solely through domestic industry in flax or hemp, in view of the
spreading machine spinning and weaving. Spinning and weaving are now to be
regarded only as supplementary work. On the whole, however, the linen
industry in Lippe was formerly also less significant than in Ravensberg,
Minden, Osnabrück, and so forth, for which reason the decline of the German
linen trade has not had such thoroughly harmful consequences there as in
the regions named, where the landless population was not seldom entirely
dependent on earnings from spinning and weaving and where, under changed
conditions, work replacing this domestic industry has not yet been found
and probably will not be found. England, as is well known, has excluded us
almost everywhere from the linen trade, so that in the end only Cuba
remained for the sale of our products, into which, however, it has likewise
already pressed its way. With the ever further expanded production of
cotton in America, it has at the same time come about that even in the West
Indies cotton fabrics have in part displaced linen ones. This, however, can
always be the case only to a certain degree, since for tropical countries
there is no more suitable clothing than the very durable and at the same
time cooling linen cloth. Therefore it is in any case to be expected that,
despite the machines, a more favorable time will come for the domestic
linen industry, once the political conditions of tropical America have
become more settled and if we do not neglect to enter into favorable direct
trade relations with it. On the one hand, hand-spun linen, because of its
greater strength and durability as well as its other advantages, will
maintain itself alongside machine linen; on the other hand, the industry of
the landless population will turn toward more careful preparation of flax
for the machines.

In Lippe, according to the report of one Kolon, about 20 to 30 years ago 12
pieces of so-called Kaufgarn were supplied for one Taler; at present 15 to
18 pieces are given for one Taler. It has been claimed that the difference
in earnings cannot be quite so great, since at present spinning is
generally done with two hands, which was not formerly the case. It must be
noted, however, that just as perhaps 4 pieces of yarn are now spun with two
hands instead of the earlier 2 or 2½ pieces, this yarn is of lower quality,
and one has even had to supply 36 pieces for one Taler. In addition,
spinning with two hands naturally requires twice as much flax. If this flax
is purchased, one receives 7 pounds uncleaned for one Taler, of which about
4 to 5 pounds remain after cleaning, from which the spinner can, with the
utmost difficulty, spin only 36 pieces; thus the work would have to be done
entirely for nothing if only one Taler is given for 36 pieces.

Meanwhile, even at higher prices for yarn, the earnings are so small that a
family dependent on spinning absolutely cannot subsist. Usually the flax is
admittedly not bought, but is grown by the Einlieger and small landowners
themselves; then, however, they must rent the land suitable for flax
cultivation, buy the linseed, and, at a time when manual labor is well
paid, use their labor power for preparing the flax. There is of course
still some gain if the flax is grown by themselves, since their own labor
is valued less highly than it would otherwise be paid; nevertheless there
are also many regions where flax does not grow especially well or does not
grow at all, so that the only remaining option is to buy it raw, if one
wishes at all to occupy oneself with spinning in spare hours. Not seldom
the larger landowners grow flax for their Einlieger on their fields at a
low price. Likewise, the servants of the larger landowners receive some
flax planted for them. With regard to the preparation of the flax, much
remains to be desired; however, more care has been applied to spinning in
recent times. If spinning is done with two hands, however, only very coarse
yarn can be supplied.

**b.** A very peculiar source of livelihood for the Lippe manual-laboring
class is brickmaking work abroad, or the so-called Hollandsgehen (seasonal
migration to Holland or elsewhere for work). Just as here in northern
Osnabrück the peat diggers who go to Holstein, Schleswig, Brandenburg, East
and West Prussia, and Posen are called Dänemarksgänger (Denmark-goers) —
because they originally went first to Holstein and Schleswig — so in Lippe
all brick workers abroad are called Hollandsgänger (Holland-goers) or
Friesländer (Friesland-goers), because they first went to Holland and
Friesland, although a very large part of them temporarily emigrate in an
entirely opposite direction. Lippers also go as peat diggers to West
Friesland and Groningen, more rarely as manual laborers of other kinds, for
example as grass mowers, gardeners, masons, carpenters, glue boilers, and
so forth, as is the case in the Principality of Osnabrück.

Brickmaking work has been carried on since ancient times by the lower
classes in the Principality of Lippe; for about 25 years it has passed from
the small landowners more to the Einlieger. As a result of the divisions of
the Gemeinheiten, the former received parcels which, though not exactly
very large, were nevertheless often quite suitable for cultivation and are
now cultivated by them. This internal colonization kept only the small
landowners in the homeland, since agriculture, which had formerly been
their secondary occupation, now became their principal occupation. Since
brickmaking work has passed more to the Einlieger, it has gained still
further in extent. This work is, for one thing, at home in Lippe, and skill
in it seems to be inherited from one generation to the next; therefore this
work on foreign brickyards may well be regarded as secure for the future.
On the whole, about 400,000 Taler are drawn into the country each year by
it. Even if earnings are weak in some regions at times, the Botenmeister
(official labor agents or messenger-masters) or Ziegelboten (brickworkers’
agents) appointed by the government always know how to find new routes
again. For Friesland and Holland two Botenmeister are appointed, who
conclude the contracts there and engage the workers in Lippe. In this way
every brick worker is secured with respect to his earnings; for in case the
contracts are not fulfilled, he can take recourse against the Botenmeister,
who is obligated to represent him before the authorities. The Botenmeister
make their journeys to Friesland — that is, West Friesland and Groningen —
and Holland twice a year, once to conclude the contracts and then, during
the work, to supervise the fulfillment of the contracts.

An adult brick worker earns, depending on whether circumstances are more or
less favorable, 40 to 60 Taler in clear money during the six months of his
absence. Individual masters not seldom bring back more than 100, and
sometimes even 200, Taler. In dry summers, when the clay from which the
bricks are prepared dries quickly and therefore can be fired more
frequently, the earnings are greatest.

Of lesser importance is the other manual labor performed by the Lippe
Einlieger in “Friesland”; it consists chiefly of peat digging. The earnings
are lower, but these peat diggers also remain away from their homeland for
a shorter time. In about three months they now earn 15 to 20, at most 25,
Taler; they leave in April and return toward the end of June or in July.
Their work, however, is more arduous and unhealthy, since it must be done
in wet conditions.`*)`

Although foreign brickmaking work brings the Einlieger very significant
earnings, it is said, as landowners claim, to have had little advantageous
effect on the condition of the Einlieger. Those who occupy themselves with
it do nothing else besides; they are to be regarded as factory workers who
rest from their labor in winter and then also cannot be employed in the
country. Moreover, the young fellows are too much left to themselves in
this brickmaking work. In winter they usually spend again what they earned
in summer, without thinking that they must put something aside for later
years — and precisely in the younger years. Therefore, in the long run,
those who serve among the Kolonen are better off, although here they
receive only 20 to 30 Taler in annual wages. In addition, the more varied
economic labors — and in general the intimate connection in which
agriculture stands with ever-changing nature — do not dull the mind.
Rather, because man does not work alone, but prosperity must come from
above, the immediate relation of the heart to God is preserved; this cannot
be said of brickmaking work carried on in a factory-like manner. In this
work the principle of division of labor prevails throughout, on which the
comparatively large earnings are founded. Meanwhile, if a person has the
same thing to do for six months, he must become mentally dulled; it is
therefore a quite natural phenomenon that, like factory workers generally,
the so-called Friesländer also generally appear morally far less steady
than the young fellows who remain behind as laborers on the holdings.

Brickmaking work abroad has a very special attraction for the lower class,
so that a few years ago after the Hamburg fire, when it attained its
greatest extent, all the young fellows from the Einlieger class emigrated
temporarily. The consequence was that the Lippe holding owners for the most
part had to draw, and still have to draw, their servants from neighboring
Prussian Westphalia, from where this brickmaking work is not practiced.
This too has contributed to the increase of the landless class. The foreign
servants working in Lippe form relationships there with the girls and then,
insofar as the girls with their children are not left entirely sitting
there — which only contributes to the increase of poverty, apart from
further evil consequences in moral respects — settle there in the case of
marriage. The landowners generally prefer to take them into their dwellings
as Einlieger rather than the so-called Friesländer, who understand little
of rural work and are also absent in summer, so that no work assistance can
be expected from them. For this reason, when Friesländer wish to marry,
they seek above all to obtain a dwelling among the small landowners,
because here they do not have to enter into obligations to perform labor
and can therefore continue their periodic emigration. If such a house owner
can spare any corner whatever, efforts are made to induce him to have it
arranged as a separate dwelling. As long as such a married couple has no
children or only a few, it manages tolerably with the cramped dwelling,
especially since the husband is absent for the greater part of the year.
But if several children are added and they grow up, the family can hardly
endure such a cramped dwelling, especially in winter, when the grown sons,
who go abroad with the father immediately after confirmation, have returned
home with him. Parents and children, sons and daughters, young and old —
all must sleep in one room, which cannot fail to have a harmful effect not
only on physical health but also on morality. Usually these brick workers
are richly blessed with children. Under such circumstances the young people
cannot be suspected of anything improper when they seek as soon as possible
to obtain a dwelling of their own, even if it is still worse than that of
their parents, in order to marry and begin a household of their own. In
this way the Lippe Einlieger class has increased in a manner not
appropriate to the size of the country and its cultivation, so that the
land, as a result of this increase of the landless population, has greatly
lost in prosperity, since despite the significant earnings abroad the
sources of livelihood are still not sufficient for the maintenance of the
lower classes. We may call the country overpopulated; for the number of
landless inhabitants has increased in relation to the number of the
propertied in an abnormal way, so that the two stand in disproportion to
one another. But the cause of this overpopulation must be regarded as the
foreign brickmaking work, as may also be seen from our account.

Initially carried on by the small landowners, who found insufficient
sources of livelihood at home, brickmaking work passed after the divisions
of the Gemeinheiten to the Einlieger, while the former now occupied
themselves with agriculture. When the Einlieger had taken brickmaking work
into their hands, they increased all the more rapidly, since they now also
gained through the small landowners the opportunity to obtain dwellings,
which the holding owners could not always give them if they emigrated
temporarily, since for their economic work they needed such Einlieger as
remained in the country. The final result then was that, although the
country already in itself is to be called overpopulated, nevertheless,
because brickmaking work abroad had gained an ever greater extent, the
holding owners had to hire foreign servants, who then even became settled
in this overpopulated country. We hardly need add that the increase of
population, now that 5,148 inhabitants already come to the square mile,
cannot rise much further, and that the relation of the propertied to the
propertyless must not become more unfavorable than it already is if both
are to continue to exist at all. Already now, when two thirds of the rural
population are propertyless, these propertyless inhabitants fall
excessively as a burden on the propertied, especially in cases of
misfortune and in years of need; what then, if the population were to rise
in the same progression as has been the case since 1819, when it amounted
to 72,000? There is here no other remedy against overpopulation than
emigration, which is already taking place widely; for internal colonization
offers little or no terrain here. We shall return to this.

**c.** A part of the Einlieger in Lippe remains in the country in summer
and occupies itself, alongside its own small-scale agriculture, with day
labor on the Kolonate. Although the earnings of these Einlieger are lower,
they are as a rule nevertheless the best off. They admittedly cannot work
their way up to great prosperity, but they are more protected from
impoverishment than the wandering population, which is on the whole less
frugal and whose earnings depend too much on external circumstances. The
present rental arrangements have existed for many years and, if both
parties mean honestly, are still suitable. Such an Einlieger, who works by
the day for his Meier (holder/operator of a Meier holding; in Lippe, the
general term used here for a substantial rural holding-holder), usually
pays four to six Taler rent for the dwelling and three Taler for the
necessary garden land. In addition, he receives 1½ to 2 Scheffel of flax
sown, for which 1½ to 2 Taler per Scheffel are paid, according to the
different regions. He also obtains from the Meier his other needs in wood,
boards, piglets, and so forth. He also usually has from the same person
several Scheffelsaat of arable land; however, this is not fixed by
contract. The Einlieger is obligated, as often as the Meier requires it, to
work for him, for which, besides board, he receives 2½ Sgr. daily wage. On
some noble estates and larger holdings the so-called large daily wage has
been introduced in more recent times, 5 to 8 Sgr. daily for the worker,
though it does not appear to be precisely to his advantage. Indeterminate
services by the Einlieger do not occur. However, holding owners not seldom
make improper use of the dependence of their day laborers. It would be
especially desirable that they provide the Einlieger with better dwellings;
for these are sometimes of such a condition that it would be far more in
keeping with fairness for the people who endure a year in them to receive
four Taler as compensation than that they should have to pay this amount.

The extent to which these Einlieger who remain in the country are
sufficiently employed or not depends on the insight and competence of the
holding owners with whom they live. An active and prudent landholder will
employ his Einlieger much in order to increase the existing powers of the
soil or to awaken new ones, which is not possible without labor. If, on the
other hand, insight into the nature of agriculture is lacking, if it is
carried on only mechanically without any thought of its further
development, then, if not always outright laziness, still in most cases a
misplaced thrift will prevail. In this case a holding owner will then seek
to spend as little as possible on daily wages and will generally do little
for the needs of the day laborers, who are then all the worse off, since
because of the obligation to work that they have undertaken they must
always be ready and are therefore prevented from pursuing earnings
elsewhere.

## 3. The Improvement of the Condition of the Einlieger

Whether and how the condition of the Einlieger can be improved is something
about which much may be said; but in Lippe, as everywhere, a thoroughgoing
remedy is lacking. In regions that are less cultivated, much can be
achieved through internal colonization. In Lippe, agriculture is also
capable of higher development; in particular, here as almost everywhere,
the small rivers could still be used in many ways for meadow cultivation.
Meanwhile, agriculture cannot be developed to such a level that the great
mass of Einlieger already present could all be employed by agriculture. The
divisions of the Gemeinheiten have admittedly also had a very harmful
effect in our Principality of Osnabrück on the class of landless rural
inhabitants, the Heuerleute, because they can keep less livestock than was
formerly the case; meanwhile, it has still not become impossible for them
to keep a cow, as is the case in Lippe, where, however, in general the
Einlieger drew less significant benefit from the Gemeinheiten than our
Heuerleute did from the often very extensive Marken (common lands or
communal fields). Incidentally, the benefit that the Einlieger had from the
right to drive a cow onto the common pasture is not estimated very highly,
since in order to keep one to advantage at least 6 to 8 Scheffelsaat of
land and a meadow corresponding to that proportion would be necessary; but
there are already too many Einlieger in the country to be able to give them
all both of these things. The cows, therefore, even before the divisions of
the Gemeinheiten, were not kept by the Einlieger precisely to their
advantage. This may well be true; nevertheless, it seems to us that a rural
economy, even if only a small one, is already incomplete without a cow,
because then, apart from other advantages, the family must do without the
very nourishing and strengthening milk. In Lippe the Einlieger keep,
instead of a cow, one or two goats in order to have milk for their coffee;
these admittedly cost less to maintain, but also provide far fewer
advantages. Butter is naturally entirely lacking in the household. There
are, however, still some Einlieger places with which enough land is
connected that one or two cows can be kept, with which the arable land is
then also cultivated; but these places are rented at a high price, and the
butter usually has to be turned into money, so that Einlieger of this kind
cannot exactly be called wealthier than the others; in any case, however,
they have better nourishment.

Given the way conditions have now taken shape in Lippe, it is not possible
to act thoroughly upon the improvement of the condition of the Einlieger by
making them, as is still possible in our principality with the Heuerleute,
from a population engaged in trade and industry back into one more engaged
in agriculture; for their number has accumulated, at least in many places,
to such a degree that this is no longer possible at all. Even if every
family now wished to keep only one cow, all the arable land would not
seldom have to be handed over to the Einlieger. And even then they would
not be able to manage without a supplementary occupation through which they
could earn ready money.

Although, then, the Lippe Einlieger cannot be made into small agricultural
holders, this does not exclude the possibility that their condition can be
improved if they are given the opportunity, in accordance with present
conditions, to practice agriculture on the whole more than has been done
hitherto. It would therefore be reasonable to demand that those landowners
who wish to take in Einlieger provide them, together with a suitable
dwelling, at least a garden and in addition several Scheffelsaat of arable
land; otherwise they should not be allowed to take them in at all. Then
only the larger landowners would take in Einlieger, who in times of need
can care for them and give them opportunity to earn something, but not the
small cultivators who arose only later. If the latter now, as soon as they
can create a room and chamber, rent these to one or another Einlieger for 4
to 6 Taler and more, but give him virtually no land at all, there is always
the greatest probability that the people will become poor and, even if not
entirely maintained by the community, will nevertheless have to be
supported; for even if the man may bring home 50 Taler or even more in
clear money through his work abroad, this is still not enough to feed a
family throughout the whole year if almost all necessities of life have to
be bought for it. If all Einlieger possessed a small agricultural holding,
the so-called Friesländer would also concern themselves more with rural
work and learn it, which at present is not the case, but will be absolutely
necessary as soon as foreign earnings decline, which under present
circumstances has already happened.

Up to now the Einlieger have on the whole still had something like a
livelihood; yet the outlook is becoming darker. In addition to foreign
brickmaking work, on which the catastrophe has had an unfavorable effect,
road construction within the country and the building of the neighboring
railway also brought some earnings. But the railway is completed, and there
are not very many roads left to build, so that one cannot count very much
on these earnings for the long term either. If, nevertheless, the increase
of the Einlieger continues from year to year, only general impoverishment
can result. This will occur all the sooner if foreign earnings too, as has
already happened for several years, become smaller. The number of periodic
emigrants must then decrease — but what then, if those who remain behind
cannot be permanently and sufficiently employed within the country either,
and the holding owners also draw foreign servants into the country because
the natives do not understand agricultural work?

This would partly be remedied if the Friesländer, before they periodically
emigrate, or else afterward, remained in the country at least for some
years and served on the holdings. Then they would become better acquainted
with the local work and conditions; they would know better how to occupy
themselves in the wintertime, even if they emigrated; and the holding
owners would not need to have their servants come from abroad. The only
question is how this is to be brought about, since it does not happen of
its own accord. The simplest thing would be if the holding owners wished or
were able to give their servants wages as high as the earnings abroad;
meanwhile, with this increase in wages, many a rural household economy
would probably not be able to exist. Yet it may be assumed that even with
some increase in daily wages, many brick workers would prefer to remain in
the country. To be sure, this increase in wages would be a sacrifice on the
part of the holding owners, since they can obtain servants more cheaply
from abroad; yet in the long run they would nevertheless gain advantage
from it, since the unavoidable consequences of increased poverty will
finally fall back upon them themselves. We do not venture to decide whether
it is practicable for legislation to intervene here, as has also been
proposed. It would, however, indeed be useful if the Friesland-goers could
be compelled to remain some years in the homeland, in order in this way to
become acquainted with rural work. Meanwhile, it is always very
questionable to interfere by police measures in people's means of earning a
living.

The inclination toward periodic emigration is now predominant. That it is
increasing rather than decreasing is also due especially to the
circumstance that the young fellows like to live unbound, even if they must
perform slave labor for part of the year. But this unbound life, which they
lead more or less during their presence in the homeland in winter and for
which their earnings in summer give them the means, can only have a harmful
effect on the country, especially on the lower class. Partly necessary, but
also many unnecessary, expenses usually absorb the earnings completely.
Sufficient employment in winter is lacking, which must always be regarded
as a misfortune and only gives opportunity for all sorts of improprieties.
The steadiness in life that is so suited to rural conditions and has such a
beneficial effect on the inner person is entirely lacking among these
wandering Einlieger. Even though some young people may use part of their
earnings to support their parents and thereby indirectly also contribute to
the common good, on the whole they do little for it. They lack too much the
noble impulse toward their own lasting acquisition, true thrift, which
holds together what has been earned, and joy in the property they
themselves have gained. But if a young fellow does not already strive for
property of his own in earlier years, this will be of the greatest
disadvantage for the future. For the most part, when the young brick
workers set out again in spring, they have got no further than they already
were the year before. Their laboriously earned money has disappeared from
their hands. A glittering luxury entirely inappropriate to the lower
classes, promoted by comparatively abundant earnings, and the desire for
pleasure have demanded their tribute; but no thought has been given to a
permanent capital stock, to be increased by yearly additions. That the
general introduction of savings banks would be very beneficial hardly needs
mention; yet their existence, as we could cite examples to show, is not
sufficient by itself — the inclination to use them must also be awakened.

It will have a great influence on the class of the Einlieger if the holding
owners concern themselves with them in all circumstances of life and
themselves preserve and develop the activity that must remain proper to the
rural landholding class. Just as a holding owner, if he preserves his
characteristically rural nature, grows together with the soil he
cultivates, so also does he grow together with those who live upon it. A
reciprocal effect will take place between him and the Einlieger. He will
communicate to the Einlieger his broader experience, his more extensive
knowledge of agriculture gained through trials or also through instruction,
and will seek to lead them further through what has proved suitable and
advantageous; in return, he can gain from their sound common sense, often
developed to a high degree in the harsh school of deprivation and strenuous
activity.

Even if in Lippe the knowledge of the rural landowner may have increased
and agriculture as a whole may be carried on with more insight, and even if
outward manners have gained insofar as gluttony is disappearing more, it
still cannot be denied that, as in so many regions, the characteristically
marked way in customs, dress, views, indeed in the entire outward and
inward physiognomy, yields more or less to a very superficial polish. The
High German language then sometimes displaces Low German, although the
latter is most intimately bound up with rural life in the countryside.
Demands upon life are then increased; in general the earlier entirely
simple rural household economy is very much, but essentially to its
disadvantage, screwed upward. Luxury and foreign fashions enter in; dressed
differently, one then considers oneself to belong to a different estate;
the old tradition is lost, and with it also firmness of character. The sons
and daughters of such larger holding owners then believe they must lead a
different life and often show little inclination toward rural work, which
is not seldom left to the servants alone. This can have only a harmful
effect. Everyone who is a landholder must have gone through all the work
himself and understand it from the ground up, even if perhaps in later
times he does not personally lend a hand at every task. If a landholder and
his wife are themselves little active, much opportunity is thereby lost to
influence the Einlieger, especially also their children. These,
particularly the girls, are after leaving school, and sometimes even
before, in a certain sense entrusted to the holding owners and their wives
for upbringing, since they must hire themselves out as early as possible.
What then, if both have little or no influence on them? Must these children
not grow up undisciplined in the years when they especially need guidance
and therefore remain without firm inner support? The outward disadvantages
too are significant; for there is a very great difference whether a young
girl works under the constant guidance and supervision of the housewife or
is more left to herself. Formerly, for example, the Meierfrauen (wives of
Meier holding-holders) regarded it as a special honor to attain the
greatest possible perfection in all flax work, which throughout requires
great care. This care then of itself passed to the maidservants who worked
under their eyes. At present the linen industry is less profitable for
larger household economies than formerly, and therefore less care is
devoted to it. The Meierfrauen with their daughters often concern
themselves only very little with it. Yet in view of the great importance
which this industry has not only for the landless population in Lippe, but
for northwestern Germany in general, it must be most urgently desired that
especially the larger landowners give it greater attention and do
everything possible to produce better flax and then to prepare it with the
greatest care. But this will never and never happen if one does not carry
on flax cultivation oneself. The Einlieger, for one thing, do not have the
necessary land for a proper rotation of flax cultivation, and in addition
they cannot give flax, which belongs among the most exhausting crops and
therefore requires much manure, the manure it needs, especially if they
cannot even keep a cow. If, moreover, the flax is then prepared very
carefully, hand-spun linen will also, with respect to outward appearance,
be less inferior to machine fabrics and will all the more readily hold its
own on the southern markets in its smoothness and strength, indeed perhaps
displace machine linen again through its virtues that appear in contrast to
the latter; for machine linen cannot fully replace hand-spun linen, and all
the less if the yarn, as usually happens, is not spun from the full length
of the flax but from little fibers 2 to 3 inches long, while hand yarn,
depending on the length of the flax, consists of fibers of about an ell. In
this case, infinitely more points, hooks, and little fibers must therefore
protrude on the surface of machine linen than on hand-spun linen, which
because of its smoothness is all the more suited to retaining the heat
flowing from the body. The yarn also loses the plant gum through the
machines; for the water vapors of 80 degrees through which the machine
thread runs, and the perpendicularly standing spindle with horizontal
thread movement, dissolve the plant glue and destroy the bast of the
fibers, whereby durability, smoothness, and stiffness are in large part
lost. Machine linen therefore stands far below genuine linen cloth made
from hand-spun yarn, and for that reason its production is not to be
abandoned; rather, the greatest possible care is to be devoted to it, so
that it may also acquire the evenness of the threads and the outward
appearance of machine linen. Only if hand spinning seeks to perfect itself
in every respect will it be able to withstand the competition of the
spinning machine. Toward this perfection of the linen industry, in Lippe as
everywhere, the landowners themselves must first work if the landless
population is to be led toward it. It is therefore wrong if the Meierfrauen
and their daughters let the linen industry fall more and more, even if the
advantages are smaller than before; rather, they must take the lead in the
careful preparation of flax and will thereby influence their maidservants
in an incalculable way, these being regarded as the future principal
bearers of the linen industry. If these girls do not receive careful
instruction already in early youth in all the procedures necessary for flax
preparation and processing, then later surely nothing excellent can be
expected of them. If the Meier daughters occupied themselves more with the
flax industry, they themselves would also value linen much more highly than
is now the case, would take pleasure in acquiring it, would gladly clothe
themselves in it, and thereby also counteract the ruinous luxury in
clothing that, despite all poverty, is spreading so widely even among the
landless class, undermines the prosperity of many a family, and
unfortunately seeks more and more to displace linen as a clothing material
in the country.

Although a part of the Einlieger has, despite all unfavorable
circumstances, still remained on the whole upright, impoverishment in
general has nevertheless greatly increased in recent years, as the rush for
support from the communal funds and the increasing spread of begging
sufficiently prove. This is partly to be ascribed to the unfavorable years
that have passed; but the principal cause may be sought in the
disproportionately great increase of the landless class, to which reference
has already been made and which no longer corresponds to the measure of the
given conditions in the Principality of Lippe. We have overcome the time
when it was considered the highest wisdom of statecraft to provide an
existence for as many people as possible, although the Lippe government
still a few years ago thought it advantageous to have as many Einlieger as
possible. What matters is not that as many people as possible have an
existence in a country, however miserable it may be, but that this
existence also be salutary for them and not bring about moral ruin. If the
existence of a population is not secured by sufficient means of livelihood,
it must become impoverished, even if this impoverishment is at first still
held off by various means. The outward disorder of life then usually brings
about an inward one as well, and the human being then only too often
becomes a plaything of sin, not combated by the power of a living faith. In
this temporal life we need, in order to live, a healthy earthly ground;
this, however, has only too often been forgotten, and instead it has been
believed that this ground would surely be found by itself if only the
population were allowed to increase. But if the human being merely ekes out
his existence wretchedly from one day to the next, he necessarily
deteriorates spiritually; for he loses the capacity to receive higher
elements of life into himself. It is true that luxury enervates the spirit,
whereas need often strengthens and ennobles the inner person — through
tribulation and suffering we are, after all, to enter into the kingdom of
God — but this too has its limits. Quite apart from the fact that, where
Christian formation is deficient, the human being does not use suffering
for his inward purification, under constant want the bodily needs press
forward so irresistibly that, even if higher elements of life have been
received, they are choked, and the whole direction of the person becomes
increasingly morally groundless. The high vocation of Christianity is to
bring inner peace of soul to the human being; but how is this possible if
the continuing uncertainty of existence, the constant worry — what shall we
eat, what shall we drink, with what shall we clothe ourselves? — allows the
human being to come to no inner rest, if instead of striving after the
kingdom of God he is filled with anxious striving for bread and a blind
greed for monetary gain? In accordance with their situation, people have in
general called the proletarians in German Angstarbeiter (anxiety-workers):
the Lippe Einlieger too must be described as such; for they cannot look
into the future without anxiety, despite all their work. But if this
happens, must not morals become wild and all life impulses be lost, so that
that path which leads upward is not found?

In order to counteract the boundless increase of the landless class in
Lippe, it seems to us absolutely necessary that legal provisions be enacted
according to which all lessors are forbidden to take in Einlieger if the
garden, arable, and meadow land necessary according to local conditions
cannot be given to them. This seems to us no more a restriction of
individual freedom than when, in the interest of the common welfare, a
house owner is required to arrange his domestic establishment so that it is
not a fire hazard. Consent to marriage also should not be granted to young
people if no guarantees exist that they can live, but rather it is
foreseeable that they must immediately become impoverished. As in general,
so also with respect to the Lippe Einlieger, we cannot wish for arbitrary
and unnecessary impediments to marriage; only we must repeat what we have
already said earlier, that the distress of the landless working class has
become great in large part because the founding of such households was
permitted of which one had to be convinced in advance that they would not
be able to subsist, and by which poverty was increased to such a degree
that the greatest charity of the well-to-do is incapable of remedying it.
Compare the above-cited work, p. 45 ff.

With special reference to Lippe conditions, we add that the new
Gemeindegesetz (municipal law) introduced since 1841 does not seem to have
a favorable effect on the mood of the Einlieger. They are admittedly not
materially disadvantaged by it; rather, the so-called Einliegergeld (tax or
assessment levied on Einlieger) has been reduced from 1½ Taler to ½ Taler,
and it is, moreover, collected with much consideration`*)`; also, of the
seven Contributions-Simplen (basic contribution units), each amounting to
5,500 Taler, two are assigned to the Amtsgemeindekassen (district communal
funds) for communal road construction and poor relief, whereby a more
active life has been called forth in the communities and opportunity for
earnings has been given**); only the provision that the Einliegergeld
together with the one Contributions-Simplum is likewise assigned to the
Amtsgemeindekasse and used for public relief seems to be thoroughly
unpopular precisely among those Einlieger who still maintain themselves by
diligence, order, and thrift. They state quite openly that the most
advisable thing for them too would be simply to let everything they still
possess go, because then they would not only be freed from paying the
Einliegergeld but would also have support to expect. Even if such talk may
not be meant entirely seriously by all, it nevertheless at least shows the
mood of the people, and it must be admitted that especially in populous
places a thoroughly wrong spirit prevails among them, so that very many
already insist that they must be maintained.

If the Einlieger were appropriately incorporated into the village communal
association and were also represented according to their circumstances in
the village council, this would in any case have a beneficial effect on
their mood. It would especially have to inspire confidence if several
sensible Einlieger also took part in the council meetings in which the
appropriate use of the assigned monies is discussed, whereby the
institution of the municipal council could only gain. Probably a more
appropriate and less disliked arrangement of poor relief would then also
come about. In any case, the system followed up to now must not be
continued any longer; for the costs of poor relief have doubled in the last
decade, often in an even shorter time. In the Amt S. (district S.), with
about 10,000 inhabitants, they now amount annually to about 2,500 Taler,
and nevertheless begging has not ceased, although police officers are
specially paid to prevent it; in particular, many children go about
begging, who are thereby withdrawn from school and so often ruined for
their whole lives. It has become evident that the mere prohibition of
begging, despite the many police officers and the frequent transports of
beggars, does not help. Giving alms too would have to be forbidden; in its
place, however, a poor-relief commission should be established for each
locality, in any case not without the participation of the Einlieger,
before which all requests for support would have to be brought. It is not
our intention here to present the organization of poor relief further; in
any case, the church must cooperate in it in an especially excellent way,
as is also recognized in Lippe, where it is acknowledged that the direction
of poor relief ought not to have been withdrawn from the church, as has
happened. Such a commission would then also have to include poor-relief
wardens, or deacons, to be appointed according to the model of the ancient
church, each of whom would have a specific district assigned to him for
special care. The work by Chalmers on ecclesiastical poor relief, adapted
into German by O. von Gerlach, we would very much recommend for
consideration in this respect, especially since one can see from it how
much can be accomplished even with small means if only they are applied in
the right way. We cannot go into particulars here; we add only the general
observation that the chief attention in a well-organized system of poor
relief must be directed especially toward remunerative employment of the
able-bodied poor, whereby the available labor powers, which in isolation
not seldom perish like individual drops running away without creating
essential benefit, are to be combined as much as possible so that the labor
comes to full realization of its value. If this is not done, but one
believes that help can be given by mere gifts, then one allows the
available labor powers to grow slack instead of strengthening them, whereby
a greater capital is destroyed than one will ever be able to replace. Thus
one will often only increase the need when one believes one is relieving
it.

As everywhere in northwestern Germany, so also in Lippe emigrations to
America have greatly increased in recent times`*)`; yet despite them the
population has still continued to increase. Although the desire to emigrate
is great, the Einlieger, especially if they have families, usually lack the
necessary means. It would be especially appropriate if the younger children
of the landowners, who cannot marry onto other Kolonate, were to emigrate;
for if they enter the Einlieger class, they usually fare poorly, since from
youth onward they are not accustomed to the narrow arrangements and the
many deprivations on which the household of an Einlieger depends. It would,
however, then still be necessary to abolish the provision in Kolonat law
according to which their dowry or inheritance portion from the parental
holding is at present given out to them only upon marriage. Emigration
would thereby be made easier, and this is all the more to be desired since,
in a case of overpopulation such as is found in the Principality of Lippe,
it remains in the end the chief means of counteracting increasing
impoverishment, though this by no means excludes the use of other suitable
means. In the long run, however, these means will bear no fruit if the soil
is altogether lacking on which the labor powers of the landless population
can come to development and to a utilization corresponding to the labor. We
believe we may put forward the claim that this soil is lacking in the
Principality of Lippe and that little opportunity is offered to gain it
through internal colonization, as is still possible in other countries.

## II. Schaumburg-Lippe

In the Principality of Schaumburg-Lippe the Einlieger are in a far more
favorable situation than in Lippe-Detmold, as follows already from the fact
that it, although more fertile, is nevertheless on average less densely
populated. On 9¾ square miles there are, according to the *Zeitschrift für
deutsche Statistik*, cited above, 32,244 inhabitants, so that the square
mile would therefore support on average 3,307 people. In 1840 the
population numbered 30,000; the average increase is 1.32 percent. The
population density is therefore much lower than in Lippe, which has an
entirely beneficial effect on prosperity. It is true that the population
has increased proportionally more strongly than in Lippe; yet fewer harmful
consequences for the prosperity of the country have resulted from this
increase, since, for one thing, more sources of livelihood flow within the
country itself, and since, furthermore, the number of inhabitants by no
means stands in disproportion to the cultivated soil.

The Principality of Schaumburg-Lippe belongs to the most blessed landscapes
of northwestern Germany. It is a part of the old County of Schaum- or
Schauenburg, to which, besides the Hessian part so named, the Hanoverian
Amts of Lauenau, Lachem — now part of the Amt of Hameln — and in part
Blumenau also belonged. The Schaumburg-Lippe or Bückeburg part of the
county is admittedly not traversed by the Weser as the Hessian part is; but
for that reason it is also less mountainous and therefore more evenly
fertile. A blessed stretch in the Hessian part is the beautiful Weser
valley; by contrast, in another valley, which is bounded on one side by the
Weser Mountains, the continuation of the Süntel, and on the other side by
the Bückeberg, and through which an insignificant little river, the Aue,
trickles, the soil is too uneven and stony to give the yield that the
generally flatter soil in the Bückeburg region gives. Mountainous here,
properly speaking, is only a part of the Amt of Bückeburg, which extends in
a southwesterly direction beyond the Weser mountain range and in which,
moreover, the Harrl rises more isolated, on whose southeastern side the
healing springs of Eilsen bubble up, while at its northern foot we find the
residence town of Bückeburg on the border between mountain country and
plain. The Amts of Stadthagen and Hagenburg are predominantly level. The
great Bückeberg, distinguished by its beautiful sandstone quarries, from
which the landscape was once called the Bückigau and from which the
prince’s residence still bears its name, slopes gradually downward on the
Schaumburg-Lippe side, whereas on the Hessian side it falls steeply into
the valley named above. The greater part of this slope belongs to
Schaumburg-Lippe and forms the region in which the rich coal deposits are
found especially around Sülbeck and Liekwegen. Part of the plain into which
this slope passes is occupied by the beautiful Schaumburg Forest.

From these preliminary remarks it is already clear that the condition of
the Einlieger in the Principality of Schaumburg-Lippe must be far more
favorable than it can be in Lippe-Detmold. The population is entirely
suited to the land; indeed, much cultivated land could still be gained if
the existing powers of the soil were better used. There is still a large
field here for internal colonization. Since the land has great wealth in
fuel in the rich coal deposits, the Schaumburg Forest, which occupies part
of the plain, could without hesitation be converted into arable land. The
water power is very little used; thus, for example, the little river Aue,
which rises in the Hessian part, runs along quite wild and seems to have
almost no other purpose than to drive mills, although with its fine
gradient it could be used with such obvious advantage for meadow
cultivation. The same is true of the other little rivers. The number of
Einlieger is also far lower in relation to the rest of the population than
in the overpopulated Principality of Lippe; for only about one third of the
rural population belongs to the class of Einlieger.

The relationship in which the Einlieger stands to the Kolon is that of
simple rent; however, leaving the dwellings rarely occurs. Notices to quit
occur perhaps in cases of disputes between the two. It lies in the nature
of the circumstances that the Einlieger is the nearest day laborer of his
Kolon, while the latter performs for him all the necessary hauling services
and cultivates his rented land, or, as one says in the Bückeburg region, is
his Pflugmann (the person who plows for him). All these services have their
customary rates, and accounts are settled for them in the winter season.

There are also in Schaumburg-Lippe small Kolonen, Brinksitzer (holder of a
small rural holding, often on the village edge or common margin; not
necessarily a farm), whose situation is usually worse than that of the
Einlieger, among whom relatively well-provided people are often found, who
at the same time have insignificant burdens to bear, whereas the small
Kolonen, as possessors, are already drawn more into the general and
communal burdens.

Spinning and weaving have remained more of a secondary occupation in
Schaumburg-Lippe; therefore the decline of the linen industry has been able
to have less harmful an effect here than in regions where the landless
rural population was entirely dependent on it. Only in the Amt of
Hagenburg, chiefly around Steinhude, is weaving done for larger-scale sale;
otherwise it is done only on individual orders on a small scale. The
cultivation of flax and its preparation have, on the whole, remained very
backward. Manuring, sowing, treatment in the field, retting, breaking,
heckling — in short, the entire treatment up to the point where the thread
comes to the spinning wheel — leaves very much to be desired. There has as
yet been little talk here of a change in this treatment, to which people in
other regions are already turning.

Far less than in Lippe-Detmold do the Einlieger in Schaumburg-Lippe occupy
themselves with work abroad. Hollandsgehen for mowing and also for herring
fishing has greatly declined; the workers go more to larger construction
work outside the country, for example to Hamburg after the fire, to
Bremerhaven, and so forth, and to field brickyards in the Landdrostei of
Stade and in Brandenburg. That there is also much opportunity for work for
the Einlieger within the country itself, and indeed more than in many
regions, hardly needs mention. Mining alone already employs a not
insignificant number of people throughout the whole year. Less significant
than in the Hessian part of the former county, in the stone quarries on the
Bückeberg, are the works in the stone quarries on the Harrl. The
considerable forests and road construction also provide some employment.
One main work of the Einlieger consists of services. In the Principality of
Schaumburg-Lippe, namely, the redemption of manorial dues and services has
not yet taken place. For the larger Kolonen, the Einlieger commonly
undertake the Handdienste (manual service obligations) for payment, or, as
people express it, perform the Diensttage (service days). Usually these
larger Kolonen stand with the Dienstbauern (service peasants or peasants
owing labor services) on such a footing that, instead of them, specific
Einlieger — often on other Kolonaten and in other villages — are summoned
directly.

The Einlieger do not contribute to the communal burdens. The obligation to
contribute to country-road construction through Landfolgen (compulsory
territorial labor obligations) is redeemed annually by most Kolonen through
payment of four Contributionssimplen (basic contribution units). The
country roads are therefore usually repaired and maintained by day
laborers, who earn 8 Sgr. per day. This work too may be regarded as one of
the principal earnings of the Einlieger.

It is of very great advantage for the Einlieger that in Schaumburg-Lippe
the Gemeinheiten have so far remained undivided; their livestock is driven
onto them along with the others in return for herding wages.

If in other regions much complaint is made about luxury in clothing even
among the lower estates, and especially if glittering finery is spreading
very widely, then, by contrast, Schaumburg-Lippe, as indeed the neighboring
region generally, must be praised because here the old “deftige” Tracht
(sturdy, substantial traditional dress or costume), as one says in
Westphalia, has been preserved, which has had the most beneficial
consequences for the landless population. As in perhaps few regions, this
Bauerntracht (peasant or rural traditional costume) has defied every
fashion and all changes, even the slightest, in cut and material. In its
completeness, toward which even the poorest servant boy and the poorest
maidservant nevertheless strive, it is by no means cheap, but rather very
expensive; but for that reason it is also extraordinarily durable, and
every article of clothing that takes dirt is washable. If anywhere, then in
this clothing solidity is to be found. It is the same for summer and
winter; only, when it is cold, all the coverings are worn together, whereas
one or another is laid aside according as it is more or less warm. When all
the coverings are worn together, it is warm and yet entirely arranged for
work; light, cooling, and comfortable when the coverings have been laid
aside. On high summer days, for example when making hay, the young man
wears a shirt and his three-quarter breeches and puts on a sleeved
waistcoat only perhaps when going to and returning from the work. If wood
is hauled in winter, two shirts, two pairs of trousers, two to three
sleeved waistcoats — no other kind is known — and a white linen lined smock
are worn, and the trimming of the fur cap is turned down into the neck. In
a similar way, according to the season, the various coverings are added or
removed by the women. Among them there is a distinctive custom that the
bright red skirts used for ordinary churchgoing are exchanged for black or
brown ones at the celebration of the Lord’s Supper. A blue cloth coat with
broad skirts and large covered buttons is the man’s clothing for
churchgoing and for a funeral. Usually the coat is acquired at marriage and
then lasts for the whole of life. Lack of church clothing is regarded as
poverty. We hardly need add that the excellent, solid Tracht of the rural
estate has a beneficial effect on its inner nature and preserves the
Einlieger from many evils with which the Heuerleute in a large part of our
Principality of Osnabrück have to contend; for here, especially in the
north, the old national costume has already almost entirely disappeared,
whereby the native clothing materials have at the same time been displaced,
which has brought unspeakable disadvantages to the manual-laboring class,
now made dependent on changing fashion. We can therefore only wish that in
Schaumburg-Lippe, as everywhere where a national costume is still found
among the rural estate, it may be preserved through all times and may help
to preserve this estate in its characteristically defined nature and to
protect it from a general leveling, with which the custom and honor of the
estate disappear.
