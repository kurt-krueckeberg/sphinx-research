## I. Lippe-Detmold

According to the census of 1846 (*Zeitschrift für deutsche Statistik*, 1847, p. 1059), the Principality of Lippe-Detmold supports 106,046 inhabitants on 20.6 square miles.

In 1834 the population numbered 98,428 (97,720), and by 1840 it had risen to 104,462, so that one may assume an average annual population increase of 0.7 percent. On average, there are 5,148 inhabitants per square mile. This is a population density found in only a few German territories. If we now consider that the Principality of Lippe is by no means fertile and cultivated throughout, but rather, being mountainous, often resists cultivation, and that it also contains large forests and many stretches of heath and only small towns, we must already conclude from this that conditions of a very peculiar kind must exist there, through which it becomes possible for the land to support so dense a population, especially since commerce, like the factories, is not of great importance. These very peculiar conditions will confront us when we examine the situation of the so-called Einlieger (landless rural lodger or tenant living in another household or on another holding), who were mentioned not long ago in this journal, p. 639, as not having voting rights in communal affairs.

### 1. Origin of the Einlieger

The landless rural population in the Principality of Lippe has increased since the end of the previous century to such a degree that, just as is the case in the Principality of Osnabrück, it exceeds the landowning population, on average, by a factor of two. Just as among us these landless rural inhabitants are called Heuerleute or Heuerlinge, from *heuern*, that is, to rent or hire for one year — *heuer* meaning “this year” — so in Lippe they are called Einlieger, because they lodge in with others, that is, live in another person’s household or dwelling. These Einlieger, to whom at present roughly two thirds of the entire rural population belong, were found in the Principality of Lippe until the middle of the previous century only in very small numbers, and in some districts perhaps not at all. Until then the younger sons of holding owners, who did not have the opportunity to marry onto other holdings or to enter into other circumstances, either settled on a still-uncultivated site in a village, or they built for themselves on the Gemeinheiten (plural of Gemeinheit, common land or village common) belonging to the village, also called heaths. In this way many small landowners arose; indeed, when the Gemeinheiten were gradually cultivated, even small settlements were formed, which were then usually named after the villages or estates from which the colonization had proceeded: Hölser-, Herser-, Wülferheide, and so forth. In general, in Lippe there predominates the inclination to live together in villages and not in Bauerschaften (rural townships or farming districts), as is more the case in Westphalia proper, where the holdings are then separated from one another by greater or lesser distances. Meanwhile, the Lippe villages are not closed villages in the way already found where the Engern and Eastphalian pattern, in contrast to the Westphalian, appears in its pure form.

The smaller landowners who arose later — Neubauer (new settlers), Neuwohner (new inhabitants), or, as the vernacular calls them, Hoppenplöcker — possessed before the division of the Gemeinheiten, with few exceptions apart from a little garden land, which was probably assigned to them at once when they received permission to build, virtually no land at all. At first they supported themselves by day labor on the holdings, where they also hired out their children as servants. As the population increased further, the work they found within the country was no longer sufficient for their maintenance; they therefore sought work abroad and, like their neighbors in Westphalia proper, turned toward Holland, West Friesland, Groningen, and the adjoining regions. It is well known that in those times, when commerce in the United Netherlands was significant, manual labor brought rich earnings there, since labor was lacking. As soon as the sons had grown up, they followed their fathers. Through this temporary emigration of the working population during the summer, a shortage of workers arose in their homeland, and especially in the season when the holdings particularly needed outside help. Just as among us in the Osnabrück region, the holding owners often complained about this and also appealed to the territorial government to set limits on this temporary emigration. Möser says with reference to this that the landowners had believed the state existed for their sake. What wonder, then, that they thought the state must supply them, by prohibiting the going to Holland, with workers who would serve for low wages? Incidentally, as in the Osnabrück region, so also in Lippe, the territorial government did not reject the complaints raised on this account, but recognized them as justified. With respect to Lippe, we admittedly know of no specific ordinance to cite; but we have been told that such ordinances were issued, though without success. Matters will not have been different here from what they were in our former prince-bishopric. Bishop Philipp Sigismund already expressed himself in 1608 to the estates to the effect that complaints had been received about servants and laborers, that no one wished to serve, whereby the hereditary peasants and the burghers were placed in difficulty; rather, the unmarried people went off according to their own pleasure to Friesland and elsewhere outside the bishopric, where they could earn more in a shorter time, in order later to return again according to their own pleasure; further, the payment of laborers was excessive, and so forth, for which reason it was to be carefully considered whether a police ordinance should not be issued for such cases. Although we are of the opinion that in earlier times there were still many sources of livelihood within the country itself, it was in any case wrong to try to restrict earning a livelihood abroad by police measures, merely so that the settled inhabitants might obtain cheap day laborers and servants. It was only natural that these measures bore no fruit.

The consequence of this temporary emigration was, in Lippe, the emergence of the so-called Einlieger. In order to obtain workers, the holding owners, as the population increased, gradually began to take married persons into their dwellings on a rental basis — to let them lodge in with them — and indeed on the condition that, when required, they would work for a fixed daily wage. Separate dwellings for the Einlieger did not exist at first. The Leibzuchtshäuser (retirement houses reserved for the older generation on a holding) that happened to stand empty were used first. Later, however, special houses, usually arranged for two families, had to be built. They are now found on almost all Kolonate (plural of Kolonat, registered rural holding or tenement; not necessarily a farm). The extent to which this landless population of Einlieger has increased in Lippe in more recent times may be shown by the following example: a holding owner informs me that in the year 1772, in one village, when his grandfather married onto the holding now possessed by him, there were only five or six Einlieger there; in 1846, by contrast, when he prepared the list of Einlieger, their number had risen to 110. The number of landowners, on the other hand, was relatively small; among 39 there were only 10 larger Kolonen (plural of Kolon, holder of a Kolonat or registered rural holding; not necessarily a farmer in the modern sense), who kept four or more horses, and 10 others, each of whom cultivated 20 to 60 Lippe Scheffelsaat (a seed-measure of land, roughly the area sown with one Scheffel of seed), in part with cows; the rest were small owners, Hoppenplöcker, who possessed no other pieces of land than the portions of the Gemeinheiten that had fallen to them or even only their little gardens; in addition, there was also a noble estate there.

As a consequence of the increase of the Einlieger, one must regard the division of the Gemeinheiten that took place throughout. The Einlieger namely drove their livestock without further ado onto the common pastures, so that these, especially since Lippe as a whole possesses only little pastureland, were excessively grazed by livestock and yielded little as Hude (common grazing land or grazing right). Not without justification did the small landowners complain that the larger ones, with their own livestock and the additional livestock of their Einlieger, were making proportionally too great a use of the Gemeinheit. These constant complaints became the occasion for dividing the Gemeinheiten almost everywhere, gradually, in order to be rid of the matter. Given the dense population of the land and the small benefit which, in general, the Gemeinheiten brought in this poorly watered region, the division may have been appropriate insofar as new land for cultivation was gained through it, even though the condition of the Einlieger was not improved by it. We are admittedly not in favor of unconditional division of the Gemeinheiten, as it has mostly taken place in the Principality of Osnabrück without any need for it, but believe rather that a limited division, such as was appropriate to the increased population, would have brought greater advantages; nevertheless we readily acknowledge that in Lippe, where such large Gemeinheiten as are found here among us did not exist at all, the circumstances were essentially different. Since the terrain in Lippe is generally not very suitable for pasture, the divided Gemeinheiten, if they are cultivated, will certainly on average yield twice as much as before. Some places will of course remain that either resist cultivation entirely or yield a return only when already existing productive forces can be directed into them in order to awaken new ones.

### 2. The Sources of Livelihood of the Einlieger

The manual-laboring classes in the Principality of Lippe, among whom, in addition to the Einlieger, one must also count the small landowners who live under the same conditions, support themselves chiefly through the linen industry and then through brickmaking work abroad.

**a.** In the already cited work *On the Condition of the Heuerleute*, etc., we expressed ourselves on the decline of the linen industry in general. What we communicated there also applies to Lippe; at present, even with all diligence, a manual-laboring family can no longer obtain its livelihood solely through domestic industry in flax or hemp, in view of the spreading machine spinning and weaving. Spinning and weaving are now to be regarded only as supplementary work. On the whole, however, the linen industry in Lippe was formerly also less significant than in Ravensberg, Minden, Osnabrück, and so forth, for which reason the decline of the German linen trade has not had such thoroughly harmful consequences there as in the regions named, where the landless population was not seldom entirely dependent on earnings from spinning and weaving and where, under changed conditions, work replacing this domestic industry has not yet been found and probably will not be found. England, as is well known, has excluded us almost everywhere from the linen trade, so that in the end only Cuba remained for the sale of our products, into which, however, it has likewise already pressed its way. With the ever further expanded production of cotton in America, it has at the same time come about that even in the West Indies cotton fabrics have in part displaced linen ones. This, however, can always be the case only to a certain degree, since for tropical countries there is no more suitable clothing than the very durable and at the same time cooling linen cloth. Therefore it is in any case to be expected that, despite the machines, a more favorable time will come for the domestic linen industry, once the political conditions of tropical America have become more settled and if we do not neglect to enter into favorable direct trade relations with it. On the one hand, hand-spun linen, because of its greater strength and durability as well as its other advantages, will maintain itself alongside machine linen; on the other hand, the industry of the landless population will turn toward more careful preparation of flax for the machines.

In Lippe, according to the report of one Kolon, about 20 to 30 years ago 12 pieces of so-called Kaufgarn were supplied for one Taler; at present 15 to 18 pieces are given for one Taler. It has been claimed that the difference in earnings cannot be quite so great, since at present spinning is generally done with two hands, which was not formerly the case. It must be noted, however, that just as perhaps 4 pieces of yarn are now spun with two hands instead of the earlier 2 or 2½ pieces, this yarn is of lower quality, and one has even had to supply 36 pieces for one Taler. In addition, spinning with two hands naturally requires twice as much flax. If this flax is purchased, one receives 7 pounds uncleaned for one Taler, of which about 4 to 5 pounds remain after cleaning, from which the spinner can, with the utmost difficulty, spin only 36 pieces; thus the work would have to be done entirely for nothing if only one Taler is given for 36 pieces.

Meanwhile, even at higher prices for yarn, the earnings are so small that a family dependent on spinning absolutely cannot subsist. Usually the flax is admittedly not bought, but is grown by the Einlieger and small landowners themselves; then, however, they must rent the land suitable for flax cultivation, buy the linseed, and, at a time when manual labor is well paid, use their labor power for preparing the flax. There is of course still some gain if the flax is grown by themselves, since their own labor is valued less highly than it would otherwise be paid; nevertheless there are also many regions where flax does not grow especially well or does not grow at all, so that the only remaining option is to buy it raw, if one wishes at all to occupy oneself with spinning in spare hours. Not seldom the larger landowners grow flax for their Einlieger on their fields at a low price. Likewise, the servants of the larger landowners receive some flax planted for them. With regard to the preparation of the flax, much remains to be desired; however, more care has been applied to spinning in recent times. If spinning is done with two hands, however, only very coarse yarn can be supplied.

**b.** A very peculiar source of livelihood for the Lippe manual-laboring class is brickmaking work abroad, or the so-called Hollandsgehen (seasonal migration to Holland or elsewhere for work). Just as here in northern Osnabrück the peat diggers who go to Holstein, Schleswig, Brandenburg, East and West Prussia, and Posen are called Dänemarksgänger (Denmark-goers) — because they originally went first to Holstein and Schleswig — so in Lippe all brick workers abroad are called Hollandsgänger (Holland-goers) or Friesländer (Friesland-goers), because they first went to Holland and Friesland, although a very large part of them temporarily emigrate in an entirely opposite direction. Lippers also go as peat diggers to West Friesland and Groningen, more rarely as manual laborers of other kinds, for example as grass mowers, gardeners, masons, carpenters, glue boilers, and so forth, as is the case in the Principality of Osnabrück.

Brickmaking work has been carried on since ancient times by the lower classes in the Principality of Lippe; for about 25 years it has passed from the small landowners more to the Einlieger. As a result of the divisions of the Gemeinheiten, the former received parcels which, though not exactly very large, were nevertheless often quite suitable for cultivation and are now cultivated by them. This internal colonization kept only the small landowners in the homeland, since agriculture, which had formerly been their secondary occupation, now became their principal occupation. Since brickmaking work has passed more to the Einlieger, it has gained still further in extent. This work is, for one thing, at home in Lippe, and skill in it seems to be inherited from one generation to the next; therefore this work on foreign brickyards may well be regarded as secure for the future. On the whole, about 400,000 Taler are drawn into the country each year by it. Even if earnings are weak in some regions at times, the Botenmeister (official labor agents or messenger-masters) or Ziegelboten (brickworkers’ agents) appointed by the government always know how to find new routes again. For Friesland and Holland two Botenmeister are appointed, who conclude the contracts there and engage the workers in Lippe. In this way every brick worker is secured with respect to his earnings; for in case the contracts are not fulfilled, he can take recourse against the Botenmeister, who is obligated to represent him before the authorities. The Botenmeister make their journeys to Friesland — that is, West Friesland and Groningen — and Holland twice a year, once to conclude the contracts and then, during the work, to supervise the fulfillment of the contracts.

An adult brick worker earns, depending on whether circumstances are more or less favorable, 40 to 60 Taler in clear money during the six months of his absence. Individual masters not seldom bring back more than 100, and sometimes even 200, Taler. In dry summers, when the clay from which the bricks are prepared dries quickly and therefore can be fired more frequently, the earnings are greatest.

Of lesser importance is the other manual labor performed by the Lippe Einlieger in “Friesland”; it consists chiefly of peat digging. The earnings are lower, but these peat diggers also remain away from their homeland for a shorter time. In about three months they now earn 15 to 20, at most 25, Taler; they leave in April and return toward the end of June or in July. Their work, however, is more arduous and unhealthy, since it must be done in wet conditions.`*)`

Although foreign brickmaking work brings the Einlieger very significant earnings, it is said, as landowners claim, to have had little advantageous effect on the condition of the Einlieger. Those who occupy themselves with it do nothing else besides; they are to be regarded as factory workers who rest from their labor in winter and then also cannot be employed in the country. Moreover, the young fellows are too much left to themselves in this brickmaking work. In winter they usually spend again what they earned in summer, without thinking that they must put something aside for later years — and precisely in the younger years. Therefore, in the long run, those who serve among the Kolonen are better off, although here they receive only 20 to 30 Taler in annual wages. In addition, the more varied economic labors — and in general the intimate connection in which agriculture stands with ever-changing nature — do not dull the mind. Rather, because man does not work alone, but prosperity must come from above, the immediate relation of the heart to God is preserved; this cannot be said of brickmaking work carried on in a factory-like manner. In this work the principle of division of labor prevails throughout, on which the comparatively large earnings are founded. Meanwhile, if a person has the same thing to do for six months, he must become mentally dulled; it is therefore a quite natural phenomenon that, like factory workers generally, the so-called Friesländer also generally appear morally far less steady than the young fellows who remain behind as laborers on the holdings.

Brickmaking work abroad has a very special attraction for the lower class, so that a few years ago after the Hamburg fire, when it attained its greatest extent, all the young fellows from the Einlieger class emigrated temporarily. The consequence was that the Lippe holding owners for the most part had to draw, and still have to draw, their servants from neighboring Prussian Westphalia, from where this brickmaking work is not practiced. This too has contributed to the increase of the landless class. The foreign servants working in Lippe form relationships there with the girls and then, insofar as the girls with their children are not left entirely sitting there — which only contributes to the increase of poverty, apart from further evil consequences in moral respects — settle there in the case of marriage. The landowners generally prefer to take them into their dwellings as Einlieger rather than the so-called Friesländer, who understand little of rural work and are also absent in summer, so that no work assistance can be expected from them. For this reason, when Friesländer wish to marry, they seek above all to obtain a dwelling among the small landowners, because here they do not have to enter into obligations to perform labor and can therefore continue their periodic emigration. If such a house owner can spare any corner whatever, efforts are made to induce him to have it arranged as a separate dwelling. As long as such a married couple has no children or only a few, it manages tolerably with the cramped dwelling, especially since the husband is absent for the greater part of the year. But if several children are added and they grow up, the family can hardly endure such a cramped dwelling, especially in winter, when the grown sons, who go abroad with the father immediately after confirmation, have returned home with him. Parents and children, sons and daughters, young and old — all must sleep in one room, which cannot fail to have a harmful effect not only on physical health but also on morality. Usually these brick workers are richly blessed with children. Under such circumstances the young people cannot be suspected of anything improper when they seek as soon as possible to obtain a dwelling of their own, even if it is still worse than that of their parents, in order to marry and begin a household of their own. In this way the Lippe Einlieger class has increased in a manner not appropriate to the size of the country and its cultivation, so that the land, as a result of this increase of the landless population, has greatly lost in prosperity, since despite the significant earnings abroad the sources of livelihood are still not sufficient for the maintenance of the lower classes. We may call the country overpopulated; for the number of landless inhabitants has increased in relation to the number of the propertied in an abnormal way, so that the two stand in disproportion to one another. But the cause of this overpopulation must be regarded as the foreign brickmaking work, as may also be seen from our account.

Initially carried on by the small landowners, who found insufficient sources of livelihood at home, brickmaking work passed after the divisions of the Gemeinheiten to the Einlieger, while the former now occupied themselves with agriculture. When the Einlieger had taken brickmaking work into their hands, they increased all the more rapidly, since they now also gained through the small landowners the opportunity to obtain dwellings, which the holding owners could not always give them if they emigrated temporarily, since for their economic work they needed such Einlieger as remained in the country. The final result then was that, although the country already in itself is to be called overpopulated, nevertheless, because brickmaking work abroad had gained an ever greater extent, the holding owners had to hire foreign servants, who then even became settled in this overpopulated country. We hardly need add that the increase of population, now that 5,148 inhabitants already come to the square mile, cannot rise much further, and that the relation of the propertied to the propertyless must not become more unfavorable than it already is if both are to continue to exist at all. Already now, when two thirds of the rural population are propertyless, these propertyless inhabitants fall excessively as a burden on the propertied, especially in cases of misfortune and in years of need; what then, if the population were to rise in the same progression as has been the case since 1819, when it amounted to 72,000? There is here no other remedy against overpopulation than emigration, which is already taking place widely; for internal colonization offers little or no terrain here. We shall return to this.

**c.** A part of the Einlieger in Lippe remains in the country in summer and occupies itself, alongside its own small-scale agriculture, with day labor on the Kolonate. Although the earnings of these Einlieger are lower, they are as a rule nevertheless the best off. They admittedly cannot work their way up to great prosperity, but they are more protected from impoverishment than the wandering population, which is on the whole less frugal and whose earnings depend too much on external circumstances. The present rental arrangements have existed for many years and, if both parties mean honestly, are still suitable. Such an Einlieger, who works by the day for his Meier (holder/operator of a Meier holding; in Lippe, the general term used here for a substantial rural holding-holder), usually pays four to six Taler rent for the dwelling and three Taler for the necessary garden land. In addition, he receives 1½ to 2 Scheffel of flax sown, for which 1½ to 2 Taler per Scheffel are paid, according to the different regions. He also obtains from the Meier his other needs in wood, boards, piglets, and so forth. He also usually has from the same person several Scheffelsaat of arable land; however, this is not fixed by contract. The Einlieger is obligated, as often as the Meier requires it, to work for him, for which, besides board, he receives 2½ Sgr. daily wage. On some noble estates and larger holdings the so-called large daily wage has been introduced in more recent times, 5 to 8 Sgr. daily for the worker, though it does not appear to be precisely to his advantage. Indeterminate services by the Einlieger do not occur. However, holding owners not seldom make improper use of the dependence of their day laborers. It would be especially desirable that they provide the Einlieger with better dwellings; for these are sometimes of such a condition that it would be far more in keeping with fairness for the people who endure a year in them to receive four Taler as compensation than that they should have to pay this amount.

The extent to which these Einlieger who remain in the country are sufficiently employed or not depends on the insight and competence of the holding owners with whom they live. An active and prudent landholder will employ his Einlieger much in order to increase the existing powers of the soil or to awaken new ones, which is not possible without labor. If, on the other hand, insight into the nature of agriculture is lacking, if it is carried on only mechanically without any thought of its further development, then, if not always outright laziness, still in most cases a misplaced thrift will prevail. In this case a holding owner will then seek to spend as little as possible on daily wages and will generally do little for the needs of the day laborers, who are then all the worse off, since because of the obligation to work that they have undertaken they must always be ready and are therefore prevented from pursuing earnings elsewhere.
