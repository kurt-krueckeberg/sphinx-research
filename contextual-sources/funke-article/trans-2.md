## II. Schaumburg-Lippe

In the Principality of Schaumburg-Lippe the Einlieger are in a far more
favorable situation than in Lippe-Detmold, as follows already from the fact
that it, although more fertile, is nevertheless on average less densely
populated. On 9¾ square miles there are, according to the *Zeitschrift für
deutsche Statistik*, cited above, 32,244 inhabitants, so that the square
mile would therefore support on average 3,307 people. In 1840 the
population numbered 30,000; the average increase is 1.32 percent. The
population density is therefore much lower than in Lippe, which has an
entirely beneficial effect on prosperity. It is true that the population
has increased proportionally more strongly than in Lippe; yet fewer harmful
consequences for the prosperity of the country have resulted from this
increase, since, for one thing, more sources of livelihood flow within the
country itself, and since, furthermore, the number of inhabitants by no
means stands in disproportion to the cultivated soil.

The Principality of Schaumburg-Lippe belongs to the most blessed landscapes
of northwestern Germany. It is a part of the old County of Schaum- or
Schauenburg, to which, besides the Hessian part so named, the Hanoverian
Amts of Lauenau, Lachem — now part of the Amt of Hameln — and in part
Blumenau also belonged. The Schaumburg-Lippe or Bückeburg part of the
county is admittedly not traversed by the Weser as the Hessian part is; but
for that reason it is also less mountainous and therefore more evenly
fertile. A blessed stretch in the Hessian part is the beautiful Weser
valley; by contrast, in another valley, which is bounded on one side by the
Weser Mountains, the continuation of the Süntel, and on the other side by
the Bückeberg, and through which an insignificant little river, the Aue,
trickles, the soil is too uneven and stony to give the yield that the
generally flatter soil in the Bückeburg region gives. Mountainous here,
properly speaking, is only a part of the Amt of Bückeburg, which extends in
a southwesterly direction beyond the Weser mountain range and in which,
moreover, the Harrl rises more isolated, on whose southeastern side the
healing springs of Eilsen bubble up, while at its northern foot we find the
residence town of Bückeburg on the border between mountain country and
plain. The Amts of Stadthagen and Hagenburg are predominantly level. The
great Bückeberg, distinguished by its beautiful sandstone quarries, from
which the landscape was once called the Bückigau and from which the
prince’s residence still bears its name, slopes gradually downward on the
Schaumburg-Lippe side, whereas on the Hessian side it falls steeply into
the valley named above. The greater part of this slope belongs to
Schaumburg-Lippe and forms the region in which the rich coal deposits are
found especially around Sülbeck and Liekwegen. Part of the plain into which
this slope passes is occupied by the beautiful Schaumburg Forest.

From these preliminary remarks it is already clear that the condition of
the Einlieger in the Principality of Schaumburg-Lippe must be far more
favorable than it can be in Lippe-Detmold. The population is entirely
suited to the land; indeed, much cultivated land could still be gained if
the existing powers of the soil were better used. There is still a large
field here for internal colonization. Since the land has great wealth in
fuel in the rich coal deposits, the Schaumburg Forest, which occupies part
of the plain, could without hesitation be converted into arable land. The
water power is very little used; thus, for example, the little river Aue,
which rises in the Hessian part, runs along quite wild and seems to have
almost no other purpose than to drive mills, although with its fine
gradient it could be used with such obvious advantage for meadow
cultivation. The same is true of the other little rivers. The number of
Einlieger is also far lower in relation to the rest of the population than
in the overpopulated Principality of Lippe; for only about one third of the
rural population belongs to the class of Einlieger.

The relationship in which the Einlieger stand to the Kolon is that of
simple rent; however, leaving the dwellings rarely occurs. Dismissals or
notices to quit occur perhaps in cases of disputes between the two. It lies
in the nature of the circumstances that the Einlieger is the nearest day
laborer of his Kolon, while the latter performs for him all the necessary
hauling services and cultivates his rented land, or, as one says in the
Bückeburg region, is his Pflugmann, that is, the man who plows for him. All
these services have their customary rates, and accounts are settled for
them in the winter season.

There are also in Schaumburg-Lippe small Kolonen, Brinksitzer, whose
situation is usually worse than that of the Einlieger, among whom
relatively well-provided people are often found, who at the same time have
insignificant burdens to bear, whereas the small Kolonen, as owners, are
already drawn more into the general and communal burdens.

Spinning and weaving have remained more of a secondary occupation in
Schaumburg-Lippe; therefore the decline of the linen industry has been able
to have less harmful an effect here than in regions where the landless
rural population was entirely dependent on it. Only in the Amt of
Hagenburg, chiefly around Steinhude, is weaving done for larger-scale sale;
otherwise it is done only on individual orders on a small scale. The
cultivation of flax and its preparation have, on the whole, remained very
backward. Manuring, sowing, treatment in the field, retting, breaking,
heckling — in short, the entire treatment up to the point where the fiber
comes to the spinning wheel — leaves very much to be desired. There has as
yet been little talk here of a change in this treatment, to which people in
other regions are already turning.

Far less than in Lippe-Detmold do the Einlieger in Schaumburg-Lippe occupy
themselves with work abroad. Hollandsgehen, the going to Holland, for
mowing and also for herring fishing has greatly declined; the workers go
more to larger construction work outside the country, for example to
Hamburg after the fire, to Bremerhaven, and so forth, and to field
brickyards in the Landdrostei of Stade and in Brandenburg. That there is
also much opportunity for work for the Einlieger within the country itself,
and indeed more than in many regions, hardly needs mention. Mining alone
already employs a not insignificant number of people throughout the whole
year. Less significant than in the Hessian part of the former county, in
the stone quarries on the Bückeberg, are the works in the stone quarries on
the Harrl. The considerable forests and road construction also provide some
employment. One main work of the Einlieger consists of services. In the
Principality of Schaumburg-Lippe, namely, the redemption of manorial dues
and services has not yet taken place. For the larger Kolonen, the Einlieger
commonly undertake the hand services for payment, or, as people express it,
perform the service days. Usually these larger Kolonen stand with the
Dienstbauern, the service peasants, on such a footing that, instead of
them, specific Einlieger — often on other Kolonate and in other villages —
are summoned directly.

The Einlieger do not contribute to the communal burdens. The obligation to
contribute to country-road construction through Landfolgen, that is,
compulsory territorial labor obligations, is redeemed annually by most
Kolonen through payment of four Contributions-Simplen. The country roads
are therefore usually repaired and maintained by day laborers, who earn 8
Sgr. per day. This work too may be regarded as one of the chief earnings of
the Einlieger.

It is of very great advantage for the Einlieger that in Schaumburg-Lippe
the commons have so far remained undivided; their livestock is driven onto
them along with the others in return for herding wages.

If in other regions much complaint is made about luxury in clothing even
among the lower orders, and especially if glittering finery has greatly
increased, then, by contrast, Schaumburg-Lippe, as indeed the neighboring
region generally, must be praised because here the old “deftige” dress, as
one says in Westphalia — that is, the sturdy, substantial traditional
costume — has been preserved, which has had the most beneficial
consequences for the landless population. As in perhaps few regions, this
peasant costume has defied every fashion and all changes, even the
slightest, in cut and material. In its completeness, toward which even the
poorest farmhand and the poorest maidservant nevertheless strive, it is by
no means cheap, but rather very expensive; but it is also extraordinarily
durable, and every article of clothing that takes dirt is washable. If
anywhere, then in this clothing solidity is to be found. It is the same for
summer and winter; only, when it is cold, all the coverings are worn
together, whereas one or another is laid aside according as it is more or
less warm. When all the coverings are worn together, it is warm and yet
entirely arranged for work; light, cooling, and comfortable when the
coverings have been removed. On high summer days, for example when making
hay, the young man wears a shirt and his three-quarter breeches, and
perhaps puts on a sleeved waistcoat only when going to and returning from
the work. If wood is hauled in winter, two shirts, two pairs of trousers,
two to three sleeved waistcoats — no other kind is known — and a white
linen lined smock are worn, and the trimming of the fur cap is turned down
into the neck. In a similar way, according to the season, the various
coverings are added or removed by the women. Among them there is a
distinctive custom that the bright red skirts used for ordinary churchgoing
are exchanged for black or brown ones at the celebration of the Lord’s
Supper. A blue cloth coat with broad skirts and large covered buttons is
the man’s clothing for churchgoing and for a funeral. Usually the coat is
acquired at marriage and then lasts for the whole of life. Lack of church
clothing is regarded as poverty. We hardly need add that the excellent,
solid costume of the peasant class has a beneficial effect on its inner
nature and preserves the Einlieger from many evils with which the
Heuerleute in a large part of our Principality of Osnabrück have to
contend; for here, especially in the north, the old national costume has
already almost entirely disappeared, whereby the native clothing materials
have at the same time been displaced, which has brought unspeakable
disadvantages to the manual-laboring class, now made dependent on changing
fashion. We can therefore only wish that in Schaumburg-Lippe, as everywhere
where a national costume is still found among the peasant class, it may be
preserved through all times and may help to preserve this class in its
characteristically defined nature and to protect it from a general
leveling, with which the custom and honor of the estate disappear.
